## Part 4: Discovery Call Script

> The goal of discovery is to find real pain and qualify the buyer — NOT to pitch or demo. Prospects should talk 70% of the call.

### Pre-Call Research (5 minutes before every call)

- [ ] Review company LinkedIn page — what do they do, what's their stage?
- [ ] Review prospect's LinkedIn profile — tenure, background, recent posts
- [ ] Check for trigger events: recent funding announcement, new exec hire, job postings
- [ ] Note 1 personalization hook to open with

### Call Structure

**Opening (2 minutes):**
> "Thanks for making time today. Before I share anything about [your company], I'd love to understand your world first — is that okay? I've got about [X] minutes for questions and then we can decide if it makes sense to go deeper."

*Why: Sets expectations, shows respect for their time, positions you as consultative, not pitchy.*

---

**Problem Discovery Questions (15–20 minutes):**

Ask 3–4 of these, then dig into the ones that generate heat (emotion, specificity, urgency):

1. **Situation:** "Tell me about how [relevant workflow] works today for your team. Walk me through a typical [week/month]."
   - *Listen for: manual processes, workarounds, tools they've cobbled together*

2. **Pain:** "What's the most frustrating part of [that workflow]? What takes longer than it should?"
   - *Listen for: time cost, error rate, missed deadlines, team friction*

3. **Impact:** "When [the pain they named] happens, what does that cost you? In time, in dollars, in team morale?"
   - *Listen for: quantifiable cost — this becomes your ROI proof point*

4. **Trigger:** "What made you take my call today? Has something changed recently?"
   - *Listen for: the trigger event — urgency signals*

5. **Tried before:** "Have you tried to solve this before? What happened?"
   - *Listen for: failed alternatives — tells you what to avoid and what to emphasize*

6. **Vision of success:** "If you could wave a magic wand, what would [this process] look like 6 months from now?"
   - *Listen for: their definition of success — become this in your demo*

---

**Qualification Check (5 minutes):**

Before ending discovery, you need to know:

- [ ] **Need confirmed:** Can you articulate their pain in their words? [Write it down]
- [ ] **Budget:** "Do you have budget set aside for this type of solution, or would this be a new line item?"
- [ ] **Authority:** "Who else would need to be involved in a decision like this?"
- [ ] **Timeline:** "When would you ideally want to have something in place?"

**Disqualification triggers:** If they have no clear pain, no budget path, and no timeline, do NOT advance. Thank them and end the call.

---

**Transition (2 minutes):**
> "This is helpful — it sounds like [paraphrase their pain in their language]. Let me show you how we approach this — I want to make sure we're relevant before we go further. Would it be okay if I shared our screen for 10 minutes?"

*Only demo if pain was confirmed. A demo shown to a non-buyer is wasted time.*

---

**Next Step Close (2 minutes):**

Never end a call without a next step on the calendar.

> "Based on what we discussed, it sounds like [their conclusion]. What would make sense as a next step? I can share [proposal / pilot terms / technical evaluation guide] and we could connect [day] at [time] to walk through it. Does that work?"

**Unacceptable endings:**
- "I'll send you something and follow up"
- "Let me know if you have questions"
- "Feel free to reach out"

## Part 5: Objection Handling Guide

> Write down the 5 most common objections you hear and your rehearsed responses.

### Objection Framework: Feel → Felt → Found

When you hear any objection:
1. **Feel:** "I understand how you feel..."
2. **Felt:** "Other customers felt the same way when they were evaluating us..."
3. **Found:** "What they found was... [proof point]"

Then ask a clarifying question to ensure the objection is real, not a brush-off.

---

**Objection 1: "It's too expensive."**

- **Reframe:** "That's a fair concern. Before we talk about price, can I ask — what does it cost you today to solve this problem without us? [Pause.] Our customers typically find they get [3–5x ROI] in [timeframe]. Is the question the absolute price, or the ROI?"
- **If still stuck:** "What would need to be true for this to feel worth it?" (Clarifies whether it's a price objection or a value gap)
- **What NOT to do:** Immediately offer a discount

---

**Objection 2: "We're happy with our current solution."**

- **Reframe:** "That's great — what are you using today? [Listen.] What's the one thing about it you'd change if you could? [Listen.] [Connect that gap to your differentiator]."
- **Challenger version:** "I hear that — most teams I speak with said the same thing before they realized [insight about what their current solution can't do as the company scales]."
- **What NOT to do:** Attack the competitor directly — it backfires

---

**Objection 3: "We don't have budget right now."**

- **Reframe:** "Is it that budget doesn't exist, or that it's not yet allocated to this problem? [Listen.] What would it take to get this prioritized in the next budget cycle?"
- **Alternative:** "Who owns the budget decision for solving [the pain you identified]?" — often the wrong person is saying they have no budget
- **What NOT to do:** Give up immediately; "no budget" often means "insufficient priority"

---

**Objection 4: "We need to think about it."**

- **Reframe:** "Absolutely. What specifically are you thinking through? Is it [option A, B, or C]?" — force specificity, because "think about it" is usually one of 3 things: price, timeline, or a stakeholder hasn't bought in
- **Takeaway:** "I want to make sure we're the right fit. What's the main thing you're unsure about?" — disarms defensiveness
- **What NOT to do:** Say "okay!" and wait passively for them to follow up

---

**Objection 5: "We're too small / not ready yet."**

- **Reframe:** "That's actually when we add the most value — when you're setting up processes before you scale. Our customers who started at your size tell us [specific benefit of starting early]. What would 'ready' look like for you?"
- **If genuinely too small:** Qualify them out gracefully. "It sounds like the timing might not be right. Let's stay in touch — can I check back with you in [specific timeframe]?" Add to a nurture sequence.
