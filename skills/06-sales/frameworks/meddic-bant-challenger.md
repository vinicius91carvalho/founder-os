## BANT — Complete Reference

### What BANT Is (and Isn't)

BANT is a **qualification filter** — it tells you whether to invest time in a prospect. It is NOT a deal management methodology. Once a deal is qualified, BANT gives you nothing for the remaining 80% of the sales process.

### Components in Depth

**B — Budget**

What to determine: Does the prospect have money allocated (or a clear path to allocate it) for this type of purchase?

Discovery questions:
- "Do you have a budget set aside for solving this problem?"
- "How do you typically fund tools in this category — opex or capex?"
- "What's the typical budget you've used for similar solutions in the past?"

Qualifying thresholds:
- **Qualified:** Has explicit budget OR the economic impact of the problem justifies creating a budget
- **Needs development:** Says "no budget" but problem is clearly expensive
- **Disqualify:** No budget and ROI case is weak

Common trap: "No budget" often means "not yet a priority," not "no money exists." Dig deeper before disqualifying.

---

**A — Authority**

What to determine: Is the person you're talking to the decision-maker, or will someone else approve the purchase?

Discovery questions:
- "If you decided to move forward, what does the sign-off process look like?"
- "Who else would need to be part of this decision?"
- "Have you purchased tools in this range before — what did that approval process look like?"

Qualifying thresholds:
- **Qualified:** Your contact IS the decision-maker, OR they can directly influence the decision-maker and have agreed to bring them in
- **Needs development:** Your contact is an influencer but you haven't met the Economic Buyer
- **Disqualify:** Your contact explicitly says they have no influence and the decision-maker is unreachable

Common trap: Contacts often overstate their authority to avoid feeling diminished. Ask about past purchase decisions to calibrate.

---

**N — Need**

What to determine: Does the prospect have a specific, urgent problem that your product solves?

Discovery questions:
- "What's the most time-consuming part of [relevant workflow] right now?"
- "What happens if this problem goes unsolved for another 6 months?"
- "How are you handling this today — and what's the workaround costing you?"

Qualifying thresholds:
- **Qualified:** Can articulate a specific pain, knows the cost of the problem, and has urgency (a reason to solve it now)
- **Needs development:** Has the pain but no urgency (it's chronic, not acute)
- **Disqualify:** No clear pain; evaluating your product out of curiosity, not necessity

---

**T — Timeline**

What to determine: Does the prospect have a specific timeframe for making a decision and implementing a solution?

Discovery questions:
- "When would you ideally want to have something like this in place?"
- "Is there a specific deadline or event driving your timeline?"
- "What happens if the timeline slips — is there a business consequence?"

Qualifying thresholds:
- **Qualified:** Decision within 90 days, and there's a business reason for it
- **Needs development:** "Sometime this year" — needs a forcing function
- **Disqualify:** "No timeline" with no urgency signals

## MEDDIC — Complete Reference

### What MEDDIC Is

MEDDIC is a **deal management framework** — it tells you how to WIN a qualified deal by mapping every person, process, and criteria in the buying organization. It was designed for complex enterprise deals where the sales cycle is long and there are multiple stakeholders.

### Components in Depth

**M — Metrics**

What to determine: What is the quantified business impact of the problem, and what measurable outcome does the buyer expect from a solution?

Why it matters: Metrics give you ROI ammunition for the Economic Buyer and give the Champion language to sell internally on your behalf.

Discovery questions:
- "What does solving this problem look like in numbers — time saved, revenue generated, cost reduced?"
- "How are you measuring [current state]? What's the target?"
- "If you could solve this in 6 months, what would the P&L impact be?"

Your goal: Convert qualitative pain into quantitative impact.
- Weak: "Reporting takes too long"
- Strong: "Reporting takes 3 days per month; at $100/hr fully loaded, that's $21,600/year + CFO is always late for board meetings"

---

**E — Economic Buyer**

What to determine: Who is the single person who controls the budget and can approve this purchase without committee approval?

Why it matters: In complex deals, the Economic Buyer (EB) is often NOT the person you've been talking to. If you've never met the EB, you don't control the deal.

Discovery questions:
- "Who typically approves purchases of this size at your company?"
- "In your last major software purchase, who signed the check?"
- "I'd love to understand the full approval process — does it go up to the CFO or CEO?"

Warning signs:
- Your contact says "I'll handle the approval" for a deal they clearly don't own
- You've been in a deal for 60+ days and never spoken to the EB
- The EB is "hard to get time with" — this is a Champion problem, not a scheduling problem

---

**D — Decision Criteria**

What to determine: What are the formal and informal criteria the buying team uses to evaluate vendors?

Why it matters: If you don't know the criteria, you can't position your solution to win on them. If competitors know the criteria and you don't, you're losing blind.

Discovery questions:
- "What are the top 3 things you're evaluating in this decision?"
- "Beyond the features, what else matters — vendor stability, support model, implementation timeline?"
- "Are there any requirements that would be automatic disqualifiers for a vendor?"
- "Are there decision criteria that aren't in the formal RFP?"

Types of criteria:
- **Technical:** Must integrate with X, must support Y users, must have Z security certification
- **Commercial:** Price ceiling, contract structure, payment terms
- **Relationship:** Vendor reference quality, executive relationships, experience in our industry
- **Process:** Ease of implementation, training requirements, change management support

---

**D — Decision Process**

What to determine: What are the steps and timeline from "evaluation complete" to "contract signed"?

Why it matters: Without the decision process, you cannot forecast accurately. Deals surprise you because you didn't know procurement review takes 3 weeks or legal review adds 30 days.

Discovery questions:
- "Walk me through your internal process once you've selected a vendor."
- "Are there any steps I should know about — procurement review, legal review, IT security review?"
- "What happens after you give the green light — who else needs to sign off?"
- "What's the single biggest thing that could cause this decision to slip?"

Map the process:
```
Champion signs off → Head of IT reviews → Procurement submits PO → CFO approves
          (Week 1)         (Week 2–3)           (Week 3–4)         (Week 4–5)
```

---

**I — Identify Pain**

What to determine: What is the specific, painful business problem that creates urgency to buy NOW rather than later?

Why it matters: Without urgency, "do nothing" wins. The pain must be active (happening now), quantifiable (costs money/time), and connected to a consequence (something bad happens if not solved).

The urgency test: "What happens if you don't solve this problem in the next quarter?"
- If they struggle to answer, urgency is weak
- If they immediately describe a business consequence, urgency is strong

Pain hierarchy:
1. **Active pain:** Happening right now, costing money or time daily
2. **Latent pain:** They know it's a problem but haven't quantified it
3. **Future pain:** "This will be a problem as we scale"

Active pain buys; latent and future pain breeds procrastination.

---

**C — Champion**

What to determine: Who inside the buying organization wants you to win and will actively fight for you when you're not in the room?

Why it matters: The Champion is your sales rep inside the account. They will present your case to the Economic Buyer, flag risks, and defend you against competitors. Without a Champion, you're hoping the deal closes — not managing it.

Champion identification questions:
- "Who on your team is most affected by this problem?"
- "Who would be most excited to see this solved?"
- "Who has escalated this problem to leadership?"

Champion development:
- Give your Champion the ROI data, the competitive comparison, and the internal business case — everything they need to sell on your behalf
- Test Champion strength: "Would you be willing to set up a meeting with your CFO so I can present the ROI case directly?" If they hesitate, they're not a true Champion

Champion vs. coach:
- **Coach:** Will give you information and stay neutral
- **Champion:** Will actively advocate for you and take risk on your behalf
- You need a Champion, not just a Coach

## Challenger Sale — Complete Reference

### Research Foundation

CEB (now Gartner) studied 6,000 sales reps across 90 companies and 5 deal types. Key findings:
- In complex sales, 53% of customer loyalty is driven by the sales experience — not the product
- Challenger reps outperform relationship builders by 2x in complex deals
- In a status quo market, Challenger outperforms ALL other profiles
- In simple, transactional sales, Challenger advantage disappears — relationship still works

### The Five Sales Profiles

| Profile | % of high performers | % of low performers |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Challenger | 40% | 7% |
| Lone Wolf | 25% | 10% |
| Hard Worker | 17% | 17% |
| Relationship Builder | 7% | 11% |
| Reactive Problem Solver | 12% | 14% |

*Relationship Builder is the MOST COMMON profile trained by sales leaders and the LEAST EFFECTIVE in complex deals.*

### Challenger Behaviors

**1. Teach for Differentiation**
- Lead with insight the buyer doesn't know
- The insight must create tension (reframe their current thinking)
- Connect the insight to your product's unique strength — not generically true

Insight formula: "[Data or trend]. This means [implication for your buyer's role]. [Companies like theirs] that address this [outcome]. [Your company] does this by [specific approach]."

**2. Tailor the Message**
- CFO: Connect to cost reduction, ROI, risk mitigation, and financial metrics
- Head of Operations: Connect to efficiency, team productivity, and process improvement
- CTO: Connect to scalability, technical debt reduction, and system reliability
- Same insight, different financial and operational framing

**3. Take Control**
- Be comfortable with assertive conversation
- Don't capitulate immediately on price — ask "what's driving the price concern?"
- Use the "give to get" rule: any concession requires a concession in return
- End every call with a committed next step — don't ask "do you want to schedule a follow-up?" — say "let's get 30 minutes on the calendar Thursday. Does 2pm work?"

### Challenger vs. Status Quo

The hardest sale is not "competitor X" — it's "do nothing." Challenger handles this directly:

**Cost of inaction model:**
1. Identify the pain (specific, quantified)
2. Calculate the annual cost of the pain
3. Add the opportunity cost (what they could do with recovered time/money)
4. Ask: "Given [X cost/year], how long can you afford to wait?"

Example:
> "Your team spends 3 days per month on reporting. At $100/hr fully loaded, that's $21,600/year — and your CFO is still getting late data. In 6 months, you'll have spent $10,800 on the problem. What would you do with that time back?"
