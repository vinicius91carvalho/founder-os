# OKR Template

> **Purpose:** Align your company, teams, and individuals around the outcomes that matter this quarter. OKRs work when Objectives are qualitative aspirations and Key Results are measurable outcomes — not tasks.

## OKR Fundamentals

**The core rule:**
- **Objective (O):** Qualitative. Aspirational. Answers "What do we want to achieve?" Should be motivating and clear without numbers. If your O contains a percentage or dollar amount, it's a KR, not an O.
- **Key Result (KR):** Quantitative. Measurable. Answers "How will we know we achieved it?" Should be an outcome (a metric that moves), not an output (a thing that ships). If your KR starts with a verb like "build," "launch," or "run," it's a task. Rewrite it as a measurable change.

**Grading:** At end of quarter, grade each KR 0.0–1.0.
- **0.7–0.9** is the target. This means you stretched.
- **1.0** means the target was too easy. Raise ambition next quarter.
- **< 0.4** means something went wrong — either the target was unrealistic, resources were missing, or strategy changed. Diagnose before resetting.

**Cadence:**
- Set: First 2 weeks of quarter
- Mid-quarter check-in: Week 6–7 (adjust if major change in context)
- Final grade: Last week of quarter
- Retrospective: Within 1 week of quarter end

## Common OKR Anti-Patterns — Quick Reference

| Anti-Pattern | How to Spot It | Fix |
|-------------|---------------|-----|
| KR is a task | Starts with a verb: "Build X," "Launch Y" | Rewrite as outcome: "X metric reaches Y by [date]" |
| O is a KR | Contains numbers or percentages | Remove numbers from O; put them in KRs |
| Too many OKRs | >3 Objectives per level | Prioritize. If everything is important, nothing is. |
| KR not owned | Team can't control the input that drives the metric | Replace with a leading indicator the team controls |
| KR always hits 1.0 | Every KR is achieved | Targets are too conservative — increase by 30–50% |
| KR never graded | Team sets OKRs but doesn't score them | Mid-quarter check-in is non-negotiable |
