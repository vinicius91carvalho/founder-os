## Diagnostic

Before giving advice, assess the founder's current operational maturity. Ask these questions to route to the right workflow:

### State Assessment

| State | Description | Route To |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Solo / Pre-Team** | Founder doing everything; no formal roles; < 3 people; no documented processes | Workflow A |
| **First Hires** | 3–10 people; ad hoc processes; founder still in every decision; no OKRs | Workflow B |
| **Team of 5–15** | Defined roles; some processes documented; need OKRs, structured hiring, board management | Workflow C |
| **Scaling Org** | 15+ people; multi-team structure; board in place; need org design, leadership hiring, investor relations | Workflow D |

### Diagnostic Questions

1. How many full-time employees do you have, and are their roles clearly documented with outcomes (not just task lists)?
2. How do you currently make decisions — does every significant call route to you, or do team members have defined authority at certain levels?
3. Do you have a board of directors or advisors you report to? How often do you meet, and do you have a standard update cadence?
4. When you need to hire someone, what does your process look like — do you have a consistent scorecard, or does it vary by role and interviewer?

### Routing Logic

```
IF fewer than 3 people or no formal roles → Route to Workflow A (Solo / Pre-Team)
ELIF 3–10 people and founder in every decision → Route to Workflow B (First Hires)
ELIF 10–15 people with defined roles but no OKRs → Route to Workflow C (Team of 5-15)
ELSE (15+ people or board in place) → Route to Workflow D (Scaling Org)
```

## Workflows

### Workflow A: Solo / Pre-Team — Laying Operational Groundwork

**Objective:** Establish the minimum operational infrastructure that lets you bring on your first hires without chaos.

**Steps:**

1. **Document your current decision logic**
   - Action: For every recurring decision you make (hiring, pricing, customer exceptions, feature scope), write a one-paragraph rule. Format: "When [condition], we [action], because [reason]."
   - Output: Decision log (10–20 rules to start)
   - Decision point: If you can't articulate the rule, the decision is probably being made by gut feel — which won't transfer to a team member

2. **Define the first 3 roles you'll hire**
   - Action: Write outcome-based job descriptions for the roles that would multiply your output most. Use the `templates/job-description.md` template. For each role, identify: what they'd own in 90 days, what "great" looks like at 6 months, and what makes someone a bad fit.
   - Output: 3 draft job descriptions
   - Checkpoint: If the JD reads like a task list ("manage calendar, respond to emails"), you've written a job description for an employee, not an owner. Rewrite with outcomes.

3. **Set your first OKRs**
   - Action: Define 1–2 company-level Objectives for the next quarter. For each Objective, write 3 Key Results (measurable outcomes, not tasks). Use the `templates/okr-template.md` template.
   - Output: Company OKRs for current quarter
   - Next: Move to Workflow B when you have your first hire in place

### Workflow B: First Hires — Building Process Without Bureaucracy

**Objective:** Systematize hiring, onboarding, and goal-setting so growth doesn't create confusion.

**Steps:**

1. **Build a structured interview process**
   - Action: For each open role, define 4–5 core competencies. Write one behavioral question per competency (STAR format: Situation, Task, Action, Result). Create a numerical scorecard (1–4 scale) for each competency. Use `templates/interview-scorecard.md`.
   - Output: Scored interview templates for each role
   - Decision point: If two interviewers would score the same candidate differently by >1 point per competency on average, your rubric isn't calibrated — run a calibration session before the next interview

2. **Define team OKRs cascaded from company OKRs**
   - Action: For each team (or individual if small), write Objectives that directly support one company OKR. Each team Objective needs 3 Key Results. The Key Results should be measurable (number, %, $) and the team should own the inputs that drive them.
   - Output: Team-level OKRs aligned to company OKRs
   - Checkpoint: If a team's OKRs don't connect to any company OKR, either the company OKRs are incomplete or the team's work is misaligned — fix this before finalizing

3. **Create a weekly operating rhythm**
   - Action: Establish a standing meeting structure. Minimum: (1) All-hands 30 min weekly — company metrics, priorities, blockers; (2) 1:1s with each direct report 30 min weekly — performance, career, blockers; (3) Async update on Fridays — what shipped, what's next, blockers.
   - Output: Meeting calendar template with agenda formats
   - Next: Move to Workflow C when team exceeds 8 people or you form a board

4. **Establish an onboarding process**
   - Action: Document first 30 days for a new hire: Day 1 setup checklist, Week 1 context (product, customers, mission), Week 2 role-specific training, 30-day goal (one clear deliverable). Assign an onboarding buddy who isn't the manager.
   - Output: Role-agnostic onboarding template + role-specific 30-day plan
   - Checkpoint: If new hires regularly say "I didn't know who to ask" in their first month, your onboarding is missing clarity on internal experts

### Workflow C: Team of 5–15 — Adding Structure That Scales

**Objective:** Introduce OKR discipline, structured hiring at scale, and board management.

**Steps:**

1. **Run a full OKR cycle**
   - Action: At the start of each quarter: (1) CEO sets 2–3 company Objectives; (2) Each team lead sets 2–3 team Objectives that support company Objectives; (3) Each individual sets 1–2 personal Objectives; (4) Finalize in an OKR alignment meeting. Mid-quarter: grade KRs on 0.0–1.0 scale. End-of-quarter: retrospective.
   - Output: Quarterly OKR document at company/team/individual levels
   - Decision point: If a team's KRs are consistently scoring 1.0 (all achieved), your targets are too conservative — aim for 70% achievement as the "success" benchmark (Google's standard)

2. **Build a board management cadence**
   - Action: Schedule quarterly board meetings. Prep a board deck using `templates/board-deck.md`. Send deck 5 days before the meeting. Monthly: send investor updates (15 min to write, 5 min to read) using the format: MoM metrics, what's working, what's not, asks.
   - Output: Board deck template + monthly update cadence
   - Checkpoint: If board members are asking you for information you haven't provided, your update cadence has gaps

3. **Define org structure and authority levels**
   - Action: Draw your org chart (even if flat). For each role, define: what they own (outcomes), what they can decide without approval, what requires your sign-off. Use a RACI for cross-functional decisions (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed).
   - Output: Org chart + authority matrix
   - Next: Move to Workflow D when org reaches 15+ or you raise a Series A

### Workflow D: Scaling Org — Org Design and Leadership Development

**Objective:** Transition from founder-led to management-led execution without losing speed or culture.

**Steps:**

1. **Design for the org you'll have in 18 months**
   - Action: Draw two org charts: current and 18-month target. Identify gaps (roles that don't exist yet), spans (managers with too many or too few reports — ideal is 5–8), and single points of failure (critical work owned by one person). Hire for gaps in the order that unblocks the most work.
   - Output: Current + future org chart with gap analysis
   - Decision point: If a manager has >8 direct reports, they're not managing — they're coordinating. Add a layer or narrow the span.

2. **Formalize the hiring function**
   - Action: Designate one person as hiring lead per open role (not always the hiring manager). Implement a sourcing pipeline: 30% employee referrals, 30% outbound sourcing, 40% inbound applicants. Set SLAs: first response within 48 hours, final decision within 14 days of first interview.
   - Output: Hiring pipeline with SLA commitments
   - Checkpoint: Time-to-fill > 60 days for non-executive roles signals a broken process; > 90 days for executive roles signals insufficient senior network

3. **Implement investor update infrastructure**
   - Action: Monthly investor updates. Lead with your #1 ask (intro to X, advice on Y, connection to Z). Structure: Metrics snapshot → Key wins → Key challenges → Asks. Keep it to one page. Send within 5 business days of month end.
   - Output: Monthly investor update template
   - Next: Evaluate if operational complexity requires a dedicated COO or Head of Operations

## Frameworks

### OKR Framework (Objectives and Key Results)

**Source:** Andy Grove (Intel), popularized by John Doerr; Liz Wessel's implementation guidance
**When to use:** Any time you need to align a team around what matters this quarter and how you'll know if you're making progress

**Core distinction:**
- **Objective:** Qualitative aspiration. Motivating, directional, time-bound. NOT measurable by itself. Example: "Become the most trusted data product in the security space."
- **Key Result:** Measurable outcome that proves the Objective was achieved. NOT a task. Example: "Increase NPS from 32 to 50 by end of Q3." NOT: "Launch NPS survey."

**Steps:**

1. **Write the Objective** — Ask: "If we achieve nothing else this quarter, what one thing would make this quarter a success?" Write it as an inspirational statement. If it sounds like a task list or includes numbers, rewrite it.
2. **Write 3 Key Results** — For each KR, ask: "How would we know, objectively and without debate, whether we achieved the Objective?" KRs should be outcomes (not outputs), owned by the team, and quantifiable. Stretch targets: aim for 70% achievement as "good" — 100% means the target was too easy.
3. **Check for coverage** — Every key company priority should appear as a KR at some level. If a priority isn't in anyone's OKRs, it won't happen.
4. **Grade mid-quarter** — At week 6, grade each KR 0.0–1.0. Below 0.4: needs intervention. 0.4–0.7: on track. Above 0.9: may be too conservative.
5. **Retrospect** — End of quarter: what scored well, what didn't, why. Feed learnings into next quarter's target-setting.

**Example application:**
> **Company Objective:** Establish product-market fit in the SMB accounting segment
> **KR1:** Reach 50 paying customers with >$100/month ARR each by Oct 31
> **KR2:** Achieve NPS ≥ 40 from cohort of first 50 customers by Oct 31
> **KR3:** Reduce 30-day churn below 8% by Oct 31

### Outcome-Based Hiring Framework

**Source:** Synthesized from Laszlo Bock (Google), Jeff Smart (Who), and structured interview research
**When to use:** Every hiring decision, from first employee through executive team

**Steps:**

1. **Define outcomes before writing the JD** — For this role, in 30/60/90/180 days, what specifically will this person have produced? If you can't answer this, you're not ready to hire — the role isn't defined yet.
2. **Write competencies, not tasks** — Identify 4–5 competencies (e.g., customer empathy, analytical rigor, cross-functional influence) that someone needs to produce those outcomes. These become your interview scorecard dimensions.
3. **Source deliberately** — Warm referrals convert 3–5x better than cold applicants. For each open role, identify 5 specific people you'd want to recruit (whether they're currently available or not), and work backward to find people like them.
4. **Interview for evidence, not impressions** — Use the scorecard (`templates/interview-scorecard.md`). Every interviewer scores independently before the debrief. Hiring decisions should correlate with scorecard scores, not "gut feel."
5. **Reference the outcomes in the offer** — When making an offer, describe the 90-day outcomes the candidate will own. If they hesitate, they've told you something important.

**Example application:**
> Rather than posting "Looking for a Head of Marketing with 5 years of B2B SaaS experience," define: "By month 6, this person will have launched 2 paid channels generating 50+ MQLs/month, hired one content specialist, and built a reporting dashboard showing CAC by channel." Then hire backward from those outcomes.

### Board Management Framework

**Source:** Fred Destin, Mark Suster, and operator experience across venture-backed companies
**When to use:** Quarterly board meetings and monthly investor updates

**Principles:**
- **The board is a resource, not an audience.** If you're presenting and they're watching, you're using it wrong.
- **Bad news early.** Investors hate surprises more than bad results. Share problems before the board meeting, not at it.
- **Lead with asks.** The most valuable thing boards do is open doors. Every update should have a clear ask.

**Board Deck Structure (see `templates/board-deck.md`):**
1. **Company snapshot** — KPIs vs. prior quarter, narrative in 3 bullets
2. **Wins this quarter** — 2–3 specific accomplishments with data
3. **Challenges** — 1–2 honest problems; your current hypothesis and what you've tried
4. **Financials** — Burn, runway, MRR/ARR growth (pre-read, not presented slide-by-slide)
5. **Roadmap update** — What shipped, what's next, any scope changes
6. **Asks** — Specific, actionable requests (intros, advice, contacts)

**Meeting facilitation:**
- Send deck 5 days before. Board members should arrive having read it.
- Open with 10-minute CEO update on state of company — narrative, not the deck.
- Use 60% of meeting time on discussions, not presentations.
- Close with explicit asks and next steps assigned.

### Investor Update Framework

**Source:** Fred Wilson (AVC), First Round Capital's investor update research
**When to use:** Monthly, within 5 business days of month end

**Format (target: 15 minutes to write, 5 minutes to read):**

```
Subject: [Company] Update — [Month Year]

ASKS (lead with this — it's what investors do for you):
• Intro to [specific person] at [specific company] for [specific reason]
• Advice on [specific decision]

METRICS:
• MRR/ARR: $X (↑Y% MoM)
• Active users: X (↑Y% MoM)
• Burn: $X/month | Runway: X months

WHAT'S WORKING:
• [1–2 specific wins with data]

WHAT'S NOT:
• [1 honest challenge with your current hypothesis]

FINANCIALS: [link to P&L or data room]
```

**Why lead with asks:** Investors want to help but often don't know how. Specific asks get actioned; vague asks don't.

## Decision Trees

### Decision 1: Is This Role Ready to Hire?

```
START: Do you have a written outcome for this role at 30/60/90 days?
│
├─ NO → Stop. Write the outcomes first. You don't have a role — you have a need.
│       Clarify: what specific problem does this person solve in 90 days?
│
└─ YES → Can you describe what "great" vs. "good" looks like in this role?
    │
    ├─ NO → You don't have calibrated expectations. Work with your current team
    │       to define the bar before writing a JD.
    │
    └─ YES → Do you have budget and 3 months of runway beyond their first paycheck?
        │
        ├─ NO → Do not hire. Hiring without financial certainty is unfair to the candidate.
        │       Explore: contractor, fractional, or part-time first.
        │
        └─ YES → Do you have at least 2 weeks to run a proper process?
            │
            ├─ NO → Slow down. Rushed hires have 3x the failure rate of deliberate ones.
            │       Reschedule non-urgent items to create time for hiring.
            │
            └─ YES → Proceed. Write JD using outcome template, build scorecard, source.
```

### Decision 2: OKR Target Setting — Too Easy or Too Hard?

```
START: What score did your team achieve on last quarter's KRs?
│
├─ 100% of KRs hit (score avg > 0.9) → Targets were too easy.
│   └─ Increase ambition: raise targets 20–40% or add a "moonshot" KR.
│       Key insight: Google's standard is 70% = good. 100% = should have aimed higher.
│
├─ 60–80% of KRs hit (score avg 0.6–0.8) → Calibration is good.
│   └─ Maintain difficulty. Investigate specific misses for root causes.
│
├─ 30–60% of KRs hit (score avg 0.3–0.6) → Possibly too hard, or execution issues.
│   └─ Ask: Were resources available? Were KRs under team's control?
│       If yes to both → execution issue. If no → target was aspirational; adjust.
│
└─ < 30% of KRs hit → Something is wrong.
    ├─ Strategy changed mid-quarter → OKRs should have been revised
    ├─ KRs were tasks, not outcomes → Rewrite next quarter
    └─ Team was overloaded → Scope down: max 3 Objectives per level
```

### Decision 3: When to Add Management Layer

```
START: Does any single manager have more than 8 direct reports?
│
├─ YES → Management span is too wide. Manager is coordinating, not developing.
│   └─ Is the work similar across reports (same function, similar tasks)?
│       ├─ YES → Span of 10–12 may be acceptable. Add team lead, not full manager.
│       └─ NO → Add a management layer. Aim for 5–7 cross-functional reports per manager.
│
└─ NO → Is any manager spending > 60% of time in individual contributor work?
    │
    ├─ YES → Manager is not managing. Either: they don't trust the team,
    │         the team isn't competent, or the role is misdesigned.
    │         Identify root cause before adding headcount.
    │
    └─ NO → Is there a single point of failure in your org?
        │
        ├─ YES → Identify the critical role and cross-train a backup.
        │         Never let one person be the only one who can do something critical.
        │
        └─ NO → Org structure is healthy for current scale. Revisit when team grows 30%.
```

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Hiring for Comfort Instead of Complement

- **What it looks like:** Founding team hires people with identical backgrounds, skills, and working styles. Team gets along great; output is homogeneous.
- **Why founders do it:** Hiring people like yourself is fast (shared vocabulary), comfortable (agreement is easy), and feels safe.
- **Why it's harmful:** Blind spots compound. If every early employee is a builder, nobody is selling. If everyone is a domain expert, nobody questions first principles. Diverse teams — in skill, not just demographics — catch more errors.
- **What to do instead:** Before every hire, ask "What does this team lack?" Map open roles to gaps, not preferences. Specifically: hire your first salesperson before you think you need one (usually at < 10 customers).

### 2. OKRs as Task Lists

- **What it looks like:** Key Results read as "Launch feature X," "Run campaign Y," "Hire Z." Team checks them off by shipping things without measuring whether they worked.
- **Why founders do it:** Tasks feel controllable and completable. "Launch the onboarding flow" is easier to track than "reduce time-to-first-value from 14 days to 3 days."
- **Why it's harmful:** Teams optimize for output (shipping) instead of outcomes (impact). You can hit 100% of your OKRs and miss the actual goal — this is how companies fail while appearing productive.
- **What to do instead:** Rewrite every "task" KR as an outcome measurement. "Launch onboarding flow" → "Reduce median TTFV from 14 days to 3 days by Oct 31." The task becomes implicit in the KR.

### 3. Founder as Bottleneck

- **What it looks like:** All significant decisions — feature scope, customer exceptions, pricing, hiring — require founder sign-off. Team waits. Speed slows as team grows.
- **Why founders do it:** In the early days, the founder IS the company's judgment. It's faster for them to decide. Delegating feels risky when the stakes are high and the person is new.
- **Why it's harmful:** Scales linearly with the founder's hours, not the team's capacity. Teaches team to ask for permission rather than make decisions. Eventually the company's growth rate is bounded by the founder's availability.
- **What to do instead:** Define explicit delegation thresholds: dollar amounts, customer impact levels, irreversibility. "Decisions up to $5k and fully reversible: decide yourself. $5k–$50k or customer-facing: inform me within 24 hours. >$50k or irreversible: consult me first." Write these down and socialize them.

### 4. Surprise-Heavy Board Meetings

- **What it looks like:** Founder withholds bad news until the board meeting, where they reveal it in real time. Board is surprised, reacts emotionally, spends the meeting in crisis mode.
- **Why founders do it:** Fear of judgment. Hope that the problem will resolve itself before the meeting. Reluctance to admit failure.
- **Why it's harmful:** Destroys trust. Board members hate surprises more than they hate bad results. When surprises happen repeatedly, boards lose confidence in the founder's judgment and begin micromanaging.
- **What to do instead:** Share bad news early and directly. Message your board lead when a significant problem emerges: "We missed our MRR target by 30%. Here's what happened, what we think caused it, and what we're doing. Will cover in detail at the board meeting." This demonstrates leadership, not weakness.

### 5. Org Chart as Org Strategy

- **What it looks like:** Founder reorganizes reporting lines, renames teams, and redraws org charts to solve coordination problems. Changes create confusion; underlying problem persists.
- **Why founders do it:** Org structure is concrete and actionable. When coordination breaks down, it looks like a structure problem — but it's usually a clarity problem.
- **Why it's harmful:** Reorgs are disruptive and expensive (morale, productivity, focus). If the root cause is unclear accountability or poor communication, a reorg doesn't fix it — it just reshuffles who is confused.
- **What to do instead:** Before reorganizing, diagnose the actual problem. Is work getting dropped? → Accountability issue. Are decisions slow? → Authority clarity issue. Are teams duplicating work? → Interface/contract issue. Fix the root cause first. Reorg only if structure genuinely prevents the fix.

### 6. Interviewing Without a Scorecard

- **What it looks like:** Different interviewers ask different questions, debrief informally, and make hiring decisions through group discussion where the loudest opinion wins.
- **Why founders do it:** Building structured interviews takes time. Early-stage moves fast. "We'll know a good person when we see one."
- **Why it's harmful:** Unstructured interviews have predictive validity similar to flipping a coin. Group debriefs without independent scoring are dominated by anchoring (the first opinion) and status (the CEO's view). You end up hiring people who are good at interviewing, not good at the job.
- **What to do instead:** Use the `templates/interview-scorecard.md` for every role. Each interviewer scores independently before the debrief. Require evidence for scores (quote the specific thing they said or did that supports the score). Hire when scorecard correlates with outcome prediction, not when "everyone liked them."
