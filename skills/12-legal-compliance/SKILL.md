## Diagnostic

Before giving advice, assess the founder's current legal maturity. Ask these questions to route to the right workflow:

### State Assessment

| State | Description | Route To |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Pre-Incorporation** | Operating as an individual or informal partnership; no legal entity formed | Workflow A |
| **Incorporating** | Ready to form the entity, structure founder agreements, and assign IP | Workflow B |
| **Raising Capital** | Raising a seed round (SAFE/note) or priced round; need investor agreements and cap table | Workflow C |
| **Scaling** | Post-seed, growing team; need employment agreements, compliance infrastructure, governance | Workflow D |

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Have you incorporated? If yes: what entity type (LLC, C-Corp, S-Corp), in which state, and when?
2. Do all founders have a founder agreement (roles, IP assignment, equity, vesting, departure terms)?
3. Is all IP (code, designs, processes, inventions) formally assigned to the company — not residing with individual founders?
4. Are you raising capital or have you already issued equity? If so, what instrument (SAFE, convertible note, priced round) and is your cap table up to date?

### Routing Logic

```
IF no entity formed → Route to Workflow A (Pre-Incorporation)
ELIF entity formed but no founder agreements or IP assignment → Route to Workflow B (Incorporating)
ELIF entity formed AND raising capital → Route to Workflow C (Raising Capital)
ELSE (post-seed, scaling team) → Route to Workflow D (Scaling)
```

## Workflows

*Note: The following workflows describe common startup legal considerations for educational purposes. All actions require consultation with a qualified attorney.*

### Workflow A: Pre-Incorporation — Entity Selection and Preparation

**Objective:** Choose the right entity structure and prepare to incorporate correctly from day one.

**Steps:**

1. **Understand entity options and select the right structure**
   - Action: Review the entity selection decision tree and framework in this skill (Frameworks section and `templates/entity-selection-guide.md`). Key question: are you planning to raise venture capital? If yes, Delaware C-Corp is the near-universal answer. If no VC financing is planned, other structures may be appropriate.
   - Output: Entity type decision with rationale
   - Decision point: *This is an "Agent MUST ask" decision — entity selection has tax, legal, and structural consequences. Consult an attorney before filing.*

2. **Identify all founders and agree on structure**
   - Action: Before incorporating, all founders should agree on: (a) equity split and rationale; (b) roles and responsibilities; (c) vesting schedule (when equity is earned); (d) what happens if a founder leaves early; (e) decision-making authority. These agreements are much harder to negotiate after incorporation.
   - Output: Founder agreement terms (to be formalized by an attorney after incorporation)
   - Checkpoint: Have you had a direct conversation (not email) with all cofounders about what happens if someone leaves in 6 months, 12 months, and 24 months? If not, do it now — before incorporating.

3. **Choose a startup attorney**
   - Action: Identify a startup-specialist attorney in your jurisdiction. Key criteria: (1) experience with early-stage companies (not just any business attorney); (2) familiarity with venture financing instruments (SAFEs, convertible notes); (3) transparent pricing (many offer early-stage packages).
   - Output: Attorney selected and engagement letter signed
   - Checkpoint: Have you received and reviewed the engagement letter? Do you understand the fee structure? Never proceed with legal work without a signed engagement letter.

### Workflow B: Incorporating — Founder Agreements, IP, and Vesting

**Objective:** Get the legal foundation right at incorporation — the moment when fixing mistakes is cheapest.

**Steps:**

1. **Incorporate and issue founder shares**
   - Action: File incorporation documents (articles/certificate of incorporation) in the chosen state. Authorize shares (typically 10 million–20 million authorized at incorporation for early-stage). Issue founder shares with a vesting schedule. File an 83(b) election within 30 days of share issuance (see Frameworks section — this is time-critical).
   - Output: Certificate of incorporation, bylaws, founder stock purchase agreements, 83(b) elections filed
   - Decision point: **CRITICAL — 83(b) election has a 30-day deadline from grant date. Missing it can result in significant tax liability. This is non-negotiable.**

2. **Sign founder agreements**
   - Action: Every founder signs: (a) Proprietary Information and Inventions Assignment Agreement (PIIA) — assigns all relevant IP to the company; (b) Founder agreements covering roles, decision-making, departure terms; (c) Non-disclosure agreement (NDA) if appropriate. Use `templates/founder-agreement.md` as a reference for key terms.
   - Output: Signed PIIA and founder agreements for all founders
   - Checkpoint: Does the PIIA specifically cover IP created before incorporation? Pre-incorporation IP is frequently missed, creating a chain-of-title problem that derails later fundraising due diligence.

3. **Assign all existing IP to the company**
   - Action: Any IP created before incorporation (code, designs, trade secrets, patents) must be explicitly assigned to the company via an IP assignment agreement — not just referenced in the PIIA. Prior employer IP (anything created while employed at a prior company) must be reviewed carefully.
   - Output: IP assignment agreement covering pre-incorporation work
   - Checkpoint: If any founder was employed during the period they were working on this startup concept, their prior employer's IP policies need attorney review. Some states (California, for example) limit employee IP assignment; some employers have broad claims.

### Workflow C: Raising Capital — SAFEs, Notes, and Priced Rounds

**Objective:** Execute fundraising instruments correctly, maintain a clean cap table, and protect founder interests.

*Note: This workflow is educational. Fundraising has significant legal and securities law implications. All fundraising must be conducted under attorney supervision.*

**Steps:**

1. **Understand your fundraising instrument**
   - Action: Review the instrument options (SAFE vs. convertible note vs. priced round) in the Frameworks section. Key distinction: SAFEs have become the most common early-stage instrument (especially Y Combinator's post-money SAFE), but the mechanics matter enormously for cap table impact. Use `templates/cap-table.md` to model the dilution impact of each scenario before signing.
   - Output: Instrument selection with modeled cap table impact
   - Decision point: Post-money SAFEs are materially different from pre-money SAFEs in cap table impact. If you're stacking multiple SAFEs, the dilution math can compound in ways that surprise founders at Series A. Model this before issuing.

2. **Conduct offering on proper legal basis**
   - Action: Securities offerings — even SAFEs — are subject to federal and state securities law. Most early-stage raises use Rule 506(b) of Regulation D (private placement exemption). This requires: (1) filing a Form D with the SEC within 15 days of the first sale; (2) compliance with accredited investor requirements; (3) no general solicitation (for 506(b)).
   - Output: Form D filed; accredited investor questionnaires on file; offering documented
   - Checkpoint: Have you verified each investor's accredited investor status? Accepting capital from a non-accredited investor without proper disclosures is a securities law violation.

3. **Maintain a clean cap table**
   - Action: After every financing event (SAFE issuance, note issuance, stock grant, option grant), update the cap table. Use `templates/cap-table.md`. Keep both a "current" view (as-issued shares) and a "fully diluted" view (all shares plus all options, warrants, and unconverted instruments). Share the cap table with investors who have information rights.
   - Output: Updated cap table after every securities transaction
   - Checkpoint: If any investor asks you for the cap table and it takes more than 5 minutes to produce, your cap table management is inadequate.

### Workflow D: Scaling — Employment, Compliance, and Governance

**Objective:** Build legal and compliance infrastructure that scales with the company and reduces risk as headcount and revenue grow.

**Steps:**

1. **Establish employment legal infrastructure**
   - Action: For every employee hire, have them sign: (a) offer letter with clear at-will employment language; (b) PIIA (same IP assignment as founders); (c) Employee handbook (at > 5 employees, certain policies may be legally required in your state). Classify workers correctly: employees vs. contractors have different legal standards, and misclassification creates significant liability.
   - Output: Employment legal document templates and classification policy
   - Decision point: Worker classification (employee vs. contractor) is a legal determination, not a business preference. Misclassifying employees as contractors creates liability for back taxes, benefits, and penalties. Ask your attorney before classifying any worker as a contractor who works primarily for you on an ongoing basis.

2. **Build a 409A valuation process**
   - Action: Before issuing any stock options, you need a 409A valuation — an independent appraisal of the company's common stock fair market value. Options must be granted at or above the 409A price to qualify for favorable tax treatment. Obtain a 409A valuation: (a) before first option grant; (b) after any financing event (SAFE conversion, priced round); (c) annually; (d) after any material event (major customer, acquisition discussion, significant revenue milestone).
   - Output: Current 409A valuation report; options granted at or above 409A price
   - Decision point: **Options granted below 409A value trigger immediate tax consequences for recipients. This is a hard legal requirement, not a best practice.**

3. **Assess industry-specific compliance requirements**
   - Action: Identify applicable compliance frameworks for your industry and business model. Common requirements: GDPR/CCPA (any user data), SOC 2 (enterprise SaaS), HIPAA (health data), PCI-DSS (payment processing), FINRA/SOX (financial services). See the Frameworks section for a compliance overview.
   - Output: Compliance roadmap with applicable frameworks, timelines, and owners
   - Checkpoint: Enterprise customers in regulated industries will ask about your compliance certifications during sales. Not having a plan for SOC 2 (for example) can block deals. Build compliance roadmap proactively, not reactively.

## Frameworks

### Entity Selection Framework

*Note: This is educational. Consult an attorney before selecting an entity type.*

**The Decision:**

| Factor | Delaware C-Corp | LLC | S-Corp |
|--------|----------------|-----|--------|
| Venture capital financing | Required by most VCs | Not VC-compatible | Not VC-compatible |
| Pass-through taxation | No (double taxation) | Yes | Yes (limitations) |
| Employee stock options (equity comp) | Standard, well-developed | Complex, uncommon | Limitations on shares |
| Number of shareholders | Unlimited | Unlimited | Max 100; must be US persons |
| Management structure | Board of directors | Flexible | Board or managers |
| Cost to maintain | Higher (Delaware franchise tax, annual fees) | Lower | Medium |
| Fundraising flexibility | Full flexibility | Limited | Limited |

**The simple rule:** Planning to raise venture capital or issue stock options to employees → Delaware C-Corp. Period. This is not a creative decision; it is a convention that makes your company legible to the VC and legal ecosystem.

**If not raising VC:** Wyoming or Delaware LLC are commonly used. Consult a tax attorney on pass-through implications.

**Why Delaware:**
- Established case law — courts have decided most disputes that can arise
- Investor familiarity — VCs and their attorneys know Delaware
- Chancellor's Court — specialized business court with predictable outcomes
- Most startup attorneys are experts in Delaware law

**Delaware C-Corp formation:**
1. File Certificate of Incorporation with Delaware Division of Corporations (~$89 filing fee + registered agent ~$150/year)
2. Obtain EIN from IRS (free, same day online)
3. Open business bank account
4. File initial Delaware annual report and pay franchise tax
5. Adopt bylaws and initial organizational resolutions

### Vesting Framework

*Note: This is educational. Vesting structures have significant tax and legal implications. Consult an attorney.*

**Standard Vesting Schedule:**
- **Duration:** 4 years total
- **Cliff:** 1 year (no shares vest until the 1-year anniversary; all shares from year 1 vest at once)
- **Monthly vesting:** After the cliff, shares vest monthly (1/48 per month) for 3 more years

**Why the 1-year cliff:** Protects against a cofounder or early employee who leaves in month 6 walking away with 12.5% of the company. The cliff ensures meaningful commitment before significant equity is earned.

**Acceleration provisions:**

| Type | What It Means | Common? | Notes |
|------|--------------|---------|-------|
| Single trigger | All unvested shares accelerate upon acquisition | Less common | VCs typically oppose — acquirer wants team retention |
| Double trigger | Unvested shares accelerate only if: (1) acquisition occurs AND (2) individual is terminated without cause | Standard | Protects employee if new owner fires them post-acquisition |
| None | No acceleration — unvested shares are forfeited | Less founder-friendly | Sometimes required by investors |

**Founder vesting:**
- Founders should vest like everyone else — even if it's their own company
- Typical: 4-year vest with 1-year cliff, or 4-year vest with no cliff (common when founders have been working together for > 1 year already)
- If one founder has been working on the company longer, their start date can be backdated by agreement

**83(b) Election — Time-Critical:**
Founders receiving restricted stock (stock subject to vesting) should file an 83(b) election with the IRS within 30 days of the grant date. This elects to pay ordinary income tax on the stock's value at grant (usually $0 or near $0 for restricted founder stock), rather than at each vesting event (when the value may be much higher). Missing this deadline can result in material tax liability. **This is one of the most time-sensitive legal actions in a startup's life. Do not delay.**

### SAFE Mechanics Framework

*Note: This is educational. SAFE terms have significant legal and financial consequences. Consult an attorney and model cap table impact before signing.*

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity):**
A SAFE is not debt — it has no maturity date and no interest. It is a contractual right to receive equity in a future priced round. SAFEs were created by Y Combinator and have become the standard early-stage financing instrument.

**Key terms:**

| Term | Definition | What It Means for You |
|------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Valuation Cap** | Maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts to equity | Higher cap = less dilution for investor; lower cap = more investor protection. Most early rounds: $3M–$15M cap for pre-seed |
| **Discount** | Percentage reduction to the conversion price (e.g., 20% discount) | Investor converts at 80% of the per-share price in the priced round |
| **Post-money SAFE** | Cap is calculated on post-money valuation INCLUDING the SAFE round | Creates a defined ownership % for the investor at time of signing. Can surprise founders when multiple SAFEs are stacked. |
| **Pre-money SAFE** | Cap calculated on pre-money valuation | Less common; more founder-friendly when multiple investors are expected |
| **MFN (Most Favored Nation)** | If you issue a future SAFE on better terms, this investor gets the same terms | Protects early investors. Standard in uncapped SAFEs. |
| **Pro-rata rights** | Investor has the right (but not obligation) to invest in the priced round to maintain their ownership % | Important for larger check sizes. Not always granted in small SAFEs. |

**Post-money SAFE dilution example:**
- SAFE issued with $5M cap, $500k investment → investor is implicitly buying 10% ownership ($500k / $5M)
- If you issue 4 more SAFEs on same terms: $2M more on same cap → 4 × 10% = 40% of company committed at conversion
- At Series A, founders own much less than expected. **Model the fully diluted cap table before each issuance.**

**SAFE vs. Convertible Note comparison:**

| Feature | SAFE | Convertible Note |
|---------|------|-----------------|
| Is it debt? | No | Yes |
| Interest | No | Yes (4–8%/year) |
| Maturity date | No | Yes (12–24 months) |
| Trigger for repayment | No — converts or returns at dissolution | At maturity if not converted |
| Standard instrument? | Yes (YC standard) | Yes, but more complex |
| Legal costs | Low ($0–3k with standard docs) | Medium ($3–8k) |

### ESOP Framework

*Note: Educational only. Option plan design has significant tax, legal, and regulatory implications. Consult an attorney and tax advisor.*

**ESOP = Employee Stock Option Pool**

**Typical pool size:**
- Pre-seed: 10–15% fully diluted
- Post-seed: 15–20% fully diluted
- Pre-Series A: VCs often require "option pool refresh" — increasing the pool to 15–20% pre-money before the round (this dilutes founders, not new investors)

**Option types:**

| Type | Tax Treatment | Who Gets Them |
|------|--------------|--------------|
| ISO (Incentive Stock Option) | Favorable — no tax at exercise; capital gains treatment if held long enough | US employees only |
| NSO (Non-Qualified Stock Option) | Less favorable — ordinary income tax at exercise on the spread | Employees, contractors, advisors |

**Key option terms:**
- **Strike price:** The price at which the employee can buy shares — must be at or above 409A fair market value at grant date
- **Exercise window:** How long after leaving the company the employee can exercise options. Standard: 90 days (this is problematic for employees — it forces a decision during a period of financial stress). Some companies extend to 5–10 years.
- **Vesting:** Standard 4-year with 1-year cliff (same as founder vesting)
- **Expiration:** Options that haven't been exercised typically expire 10 years from grant

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Delaying Incorporation Until "Things Are More Serious"

- **What it looks like:** Founders work on an idea for 6 months without incorporating. Code is written, customers are talked to, maybe some money has been exchanged — all as individuals.
- **Why founders do it:** Incorporation feels like administrative overhead. There's an implicit belief that "serious" is a threshold you'll know when you hit.
- **Why it's harmful:** IP created before incorporation must be explicitly assigned to the company — this is often missed, creating a chain-of-title problem. If a cofounder leaves before incorporating, the equity conversation happens without legal protection. Contracts signed personally create personal liability. Customers paying money creates taxable income with no entity to receive it.
- **What to do instead:** Incorporate as soon as you have more than one person working on the project or when you begin taking money. Costs: $89 filing fee + ~$1,500 in attorney fees for a standard incorporation. This is cheap compared to fixing problems later.

### 2. Skipping the 83(b) Election

- **What it looks like:** Founders receive restricted stock (with vesting), don't file the 83(b) election, and only discover the tax problem at the first vesting cliff — when the stock has significant value.
- **Why founders do it:** They don't know about it. The window is 30 days from the grant date, and if the attorney doesn't explain it, it gets missed.
- **Why it's harmful:** Without the 83(b) election, founders owe ordinary income tax on the fair market value of shares at each vesting event — not at grant (when it was effectively $0). By the Series A, shares can be worth significant amounts, and the tax bill at each vesting event is a serious financial burden.
- **What to do instead:** File the 83(b) election within 30 days of the founder stock grant. This is non-negotiable. Ask your attorney to explain it, confirm you understand it, and confirm it was filed. Get the IRS receipt.

### 3. Handshake Cofounder Agreements

- **What it looks like:** Two cofounders agree on equity split ("you're at 50, I'm at 50"), roles ("you do product, I'll do sales"), and a vague "we'll figure it out if someone leaves." No written agreement.
- **Why founders do it:** The relationship feels strong. Written agreements feel adversarial. "We trust each other."
- **Why it's harmful:** 50%+ of cofounder relationships end in departure or significant conflict within 3 years. Without a written agreement, disputes over equity, IP ownership, non-competes, and decision authority become expensive litigation. The very trust that made a written agreement feel unnecessary is what's destroyed when things go wrong.
- **What to do instead:** Use `templates/founder-agreement.md` as a starting checklist. Have a direct conversation about the hard scenarios: "What happens if you want to leave in 6 months? What if I want to leave in 6 months? What if we disagree on a major strategic decision?" Then formalize the conclusions in a written agreement signed before or at incorporation.

### 4. Cap Table Confusion

- **What it looks like:** Founder says "I own 60% of the company." This is accurate for the current fully issued shares. But they've issued 3 SAFEs with a $5M cap, have an option pool of 15%, and have one unconverted convertible note. On a fully-diluted basis, they own 32%.
- **Why founders do it:** Tracking the fully diluted cap table requires modeling instruments that haven't converted yet. It's intellectually harder than counting issued shares.
- **Why it's harmful:** Founders are surprised by their actual ownership at Series A conversion. Makes compensation discussions misleading (you can't tell someone "we'll give you 2%" without knowing the denominator). Creates negotiating mistakes on future rounds.
- **What to do instead:** Always maintain both a "current" and "fully diluted" cap table. Update after every transaction. Model the conversion of all SAFEs, notes, and warrants. Use `templates/cap-table.md`. The question "what % do I own?" must always be answered in fully diluted terms.

### 5. Using Generic Legal Documents

- **What it looks like:** Founder downloads a "startup kit" from a legal template site, fills in the blanks, and considers the legal work done. May also use AI-generated documents without attorney review.
- **Why founders do it:** Legal fees feel expensive. Templates feel like a complete substitute.
- **Why it's harmful:** Generic documents miss jurisdiction-specific requirements. They may not reflect current law (template sites are often not updated). Structural errors in incorporation documents or SAFE terms can have long-term consequences that are expensive to fix. In financing, investors will require that documents conform to their standard terms — non-standard docs delay closing.
- **What to do instead:** For standard early-stage financing, use YC's standard SAFE documents or NVCA standard documents — these ARE the industry standard and are widely understood. For entity formation and founder agreements, use a startup attorney. The cost of a proper incorporation ($1,500–$3,000) is trivially small compared to the cost of fixing errors later.

### 6. Not Assigning Contractor IP

- **What it looks like:** Company hires contractors to build the MVP. Contractor builds core features. No IP assignment agreement is signed. Company raises seed round. During due diligence, investor's attorney asks "does the company own all its IP?" Answer is unclear.
- **Why founders do it:** It feels awkward or unnecessary to have a freelancer sign legal documents. The assumption is "we paid them, so we own it."
- **Why it's harmful:** Payment does not automatically transfer IP rights. Without a work-for-hire agreement or IP assignment agreement, the contractor may retain rights to the code, design, or content they created. This creates a chain-of-title problem that can block financing and acquisitions.
- **What to do instead:** Every contractor who creates IP for your company must sign either a work-for-hire agreement or an IP assignment agreement before starting work. This is non-negotiable. Many startup attorneys will provide a standard contractor agreement template.
