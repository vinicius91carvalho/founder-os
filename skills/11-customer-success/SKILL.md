# Skill 11: Customer Success

> The fastest path to growth is keeping the customers you already have. Customer success is not support — it is the proactive discipline of ensuring customers achieve the outcomes they paid for.

## Diagnostic

Before giving advice, assess the founder's current customer success maturity. Ask these questions to route to the right workflow:

### State Assessment

| State | Description | Route To |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **No CS Function** | Founders handling all customer contact; no formal onboarding; churn happens without analysis | Workflow A |
| **First CS Hire** | One person owning CS; ad hoc onboarding; churn tracked but not systematically analyzed | Workflow B |
| **Scaling CS** | Dedicated CS team; defined onboarding; health scores being built; need churn prevention system | Workflow C |
| **CS-Led Growth** | Mature CS function; renewal and expansion motion; customers as a growth channel | Workflow D |

### Diagnostic Questions

1. What is your average time-to-first-value (TTFV) — from contract signed to the moment a customer first experiences the core value of your product?
2. When a customer churns, do you know why? Do you segment the reason by when in the customer lifecycle they churned?
3. Do you have a documented onboarding flow with specific activation milestones, or do customers "figure it out" after signing?
4. What is your NPS, and do you have a systematic process for acting on it — not just measuring it?

### Routing Logic

```
IF no defined onboarding process AND founders handling all CS → Route to Workflow A
ELIF first CS hire in place but no health scoring → Route to Workflow B
ELIF CS team in place but churn analysis is unsegmented → Route to Workflow C
ELSE (systematic health scoring AND expansion motion active) → Route to Workflow D
```

## Workflows

### Workflow A: No CS Function — Building Customer Success from Scratch

**Objective:** Establish minimum viable customer success infrastructure before churn becomes a compounding problem.

**Steps:**

1. **Map time-to-first-value for your best customers**
   - Action: Take your 5 happiest customers. Interview them (or analyze their usage data) to identify: (a) what specific moment they first "got it" — when the product delivered its core promise; (b) how long after signing that moment occurred; (c) what actions they took to get there. This is your TTFV baseline and your activation milestone sequence.
   - Output: TTFV definition with timeline and activation milestones
   - Decision point: If your 5 best customers all had different "first value" moments, you haven't defined your core use case clearly enough — that's a product/positioning problem, not a CS problem

2. **Design a minimum viable onboarding flow**
   - Action: Based on the activation milestones from Step 1, design a linear onboarding flow that guides new customers to first value as fast as possible. Minimum: (1) Day 0 welcome email with single clear next step; (2) Day 3 check-in (call or automated) — did they complete that step?; (3) Day 7 milestone check — have they reached the first activation milestone?; (4) Day 30 business review — are they on track for the outcome they purchased for?
   - Output: Documented onboarding flow with specific triggers and owner for each step
   - Checkpoint: Could a non-founding team member follow this flow without your involvement? If not, it's not documented enough.

3. **Track every customer in a simple CRM**
   - Action: Even a spreadsheet works at < 20 customers. For each customer: company name, contract value, start date, primary contact, current onboarding stage, health signal (green/yellow/red), last contact date. Review this weekly.
   - Output: Customer tracking system (CS CRM or spreadsheet)
   - Next: Move to Workflow B when you hire your first CS person or reach 20+ customers

### Workflow B: First CS Hire — Building Process and Playbooks

**Objective:** Systematize the founder's tribal knowledge into repeatable CS playbooks that a CS hire can execute.

**Steps:**

1. **Document the full customer lifecycle**
   - Action: Map every stage from "contract signed" to "expansion/renewal/churn" with explicit entry and exit criteria for each stage. Stages typically: Implementation → Onboarding → Adoption → Expansion → Renewal. For each stage: what does CS do, what does the customer do, what triggers transition to the next stage, what signals a customer is at risk?
   - Output: Customer lifecycle map with stage definitions and CS playbooks per stage
   - Decision point: If you can't define exit criteria for each stage (how do you know when a customer has "graduated" from onboarding?), you don't have stages — you have a vague process

2. **Build a churn prevention playbook segmented by timing**
   - Action: Churn at different points in the customer lifecycle has different root causes and requires different interventions. Segment your churn into at least three buckets and build a specific intervention for each. See the Frameworks section for the timing-based intervention model.
   - Output: Churn prevention playbook with specific interventions by timing and customer segment
   - Checkpoint: When a customer shows a churn signal (missed QBR, low usage, support ticket spike), can your CS hire know exactly what to do within 24 hours? If not, the playbook isn't specific enough.

3. **Implement NPS and CSAT measurement**
   - Action: NPS: send quarterly to all customers (or after key milestones). CSAT: send after every support interaction. For NPS: define what you'll do with promoters (ask for referral, case study), passives (find out what would move them to 9+), and detractors (urgent recovery call within 48 hours). Use `templates/nps-csat-survey.md`.
   - Output: NPS/CSAT measurement system with response protocol
   - Next: Move to Workflow C when team grows to 3+ CS people or customer count exceeds 50

### Workflow C: Scaling CS — Health Scoring and Systematic Churn Prevention

**Objective:** Build a data-driven health scoring system that identifies at-risk customers before they tell you they're leaving.

**Steps:**

1. **Build a customer health score model**
   - Action: A health score aggregates multiple signals into a single 0–100 score that predicts renewal likelihood. Start with 4–6 signals weighted by their correlation with churn in your data. Use `templates/health-score-model.md`. Typical signals for SaaS: product usage frequency, feature adoption depth, support ticket volume and sentiment, NPS score, executive engagement, billing health.
   - Output: Health score model with weights and calculation methodology
   - Decision point: If you don't have 12+ months of customer data, you can't validate health score weights with churn correlation analysis. Start with intuitive weights (informed by customer interviews), then validate and adjust as data accumulates.

2. **Implement segmented churn analysis**
   - Action: For every churned customer in the last 12 months, classify by: (1) WHEN they churned (30-day, 60–180-day, 6–18-month, 18+ month); (2) WHY they churned (primary reason); (3) what early signals predicted it. Run this analysis quarterly. Use `templates/churn-analysis.md`.
   - Output: Segmented churn analysis with root cause breakdown and early signal catalog
   - Checkpoint: After this analysis, can you name the top 2–3 actions that would have prevented > 50% of your churn? If not, your analysis didn't go deep enough.

3. **Build an expansion revenue playbook**
   - Action: Expansion revenue (upsells and cross-sells to existing customers) has near-zero CAC and signals product-market fit. Identify: which customers are "expansion ready" (high health score, hitting usage limits, growing team), what expansion products/tiers you can offer, and what the trigger for an expansion conversation is. Build an expansion playbook with specific offer scripts, timing triggers, and success criteria.
   - Output: Expansion playbook with account segmentation and conversation guides
   - Next: Move to Workflow D when expansion revenue exceeds 20% of new ARR

### Workflow D: CS-Led Growth — Making Customers a Growth Channel

**Objective:** Systematically turn happy customers into the company's most efficient growth channel — through referrals, case studies, reviews, and community.

**Steps:**

1. **Formalize the customer advocacy program**
   - Action: Identify your top 10–20% health score customers (promoters). Build a structured advocacy program: (1) Reference library (willing to take reference calls for prospects); (2) Case study program (willing to be featured); (3) Referral program (incentives for qualified intros); (4) Review sites (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot — ask top customers to leave reviews). Assign a CS team member to own advocacy.
   - Output: Customer advocacy program with incentive structure and metrics
   - Decision point: If < 20% of your customers would agree to a reference call, your NPS is misleading or your CS is failing — investigate before building a formal advocacy program

2. **Build community as a CS channel**
   - Action: A customer community reduces support load, increases retention (customers invested in a community have higher switching costs), and creates peer-to-peer learning that accelerates activation. Start simple: Slack community or Discourse forum. Seed with founder participation, then turn it over to power users.
   - Output: Customer community with engagement metrics
   - Checkpoint: Is the community useful to customers WITHOUT you facilitating every thread? That's the test of community health.

3. **Implement CS-to-sales handoff for expansion**
   - Action: When CS identifies an expansion opportunity (usage hitting limits, customer mentions additional use case, executive sponsor signals interest), there should be a clean handoff protocol to a dedicated expansion sales resource (AE or CS-AE hybrid). Define: trigger criteria, handoff document format, and split of credit/commission.
   - Output: CS-to-sales expansion handoff protocol
   - Next: Ongoing — run quarterly CS OKRs focused on NRR (Net Revenue Retention) as the primary CS metric

## Frameworks

### Time-to-First-Value (TTFV) Framework

**Source:** Lincoln Murphy (Customer Success), Gainsight's Customer Success methodology
**When to use:** When designing or optimizing your onboarding flow; TTFV is the most important leading indicator of retention

**Core insight:** Every day between "contract signed" and "first value experienced" is a day the customer is spending money without feeling it. Customers who experience value fast retain better and expand faster. TTFV is the most actionable lever you have.

**Steps to define and reduce TTFV:**

1. **Define "first value"** — Ask: "What is the specific moment when a new customer first thinks 'I get it — this is going to work'?" For a project management tool, it might be: "First task completed by someone who didn't create it." For a data tool: "First dashboard shared with a non-admin user." The more specific, the better.
2. **Measure current TTFV** — Pull data on your existing customer cohorts. What is the median time from contract signed to the "first value" event? What is the distribution (some customers reach it in 2 days; others take 60 days)?
3. **Find the correlation** — Do customers who reach first value in < X days retain at higher rates? If yes, X is your TTFV target. If not, your "first value" definition may be wrong.
4. **Identify the bottleneck** — For customers who took 30+ days to reach first value, what step took longest? Data import? User invitations? Setup complexity? Remove or simplify the bottleneck.
5. **Design the onboarding flow around TTFV** — Every step in onboarding should have one purpose: get the customer to first value faster. Remove anything that doesn't serve this goal.

**Example application:**
> SaaS tool for HR teams. Current TTFV: median 21 days. Analysis reveals bottleneck: getting customer's employees to complete their profiles. Fix: add an automated bulk import from HRIS (ADP, Workday) that takes 5 minutes. New TTFV: median 4 days. 6-month retention improves from 72% to 89%.

**See `frameworks/time-to-first-value.md` for the full methodology.**

### Timing-Based Churn Prevention Framework

**Source:** Derived from Lincoln Murphy, Andreessen Horowitz CS guidance, and pattern analysis across SaaS companies
**When to use:** Diagnosing churn and designing interventions — CRITICAL INSIGHT: churn at different points in the customer lifecycle requires completely different responses

**The key insight:** A customer who churns in their first 30 days is experiencing a different problem than one who churns at month 12. Treating them the same way is ineffective. Segment your churn by timing first, then by reason.

**Timing Segments and Interventions:**

| Timing | Root Cause Hypothesis | Intervention | What It's NOT About |
|--------|----------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **0–30 days** | Onboarding failure: product too complex to configure, "aha moment" too slow, wrong use case sold | Redesign onboarding; reduce TTFV; pre-sales qualification check | Relationship or value delivery — they haven't gotten started |
| **1–6 months** | Adoption failure: team not using it, initial champion lost internal support, competing priorities won | QBR at month 3 with champion AND their boss; adoption workshops; executive re-engagement | Technical issues — they're set up, just not using it |
| **6–18 months** | ROI failure: customer can't articulate the business impact, champion leaves, budget pressure | Business review focused on value realized ($); find new champion; create internal case study for customer's own stakeholders | Feature requests — they've decided not to renew, new features won't save it |
| **18+ months** | Competition or strategic shift: competitor wins on price, product-market change, company pivots | Win/loss analysis; competitive response; strategic account planning | More features or more handholding — decision was made strategically |

**How to use this framework:**
1. Classify every churned customer in the last 12 months by when they churned
2. For each timing bucket, identify the top 2–3 reasons (survey churned customers; do exit interviews)
3. Design a proactive intervention that triggers BEFORE customers reach that timing point
4. Measure whether the intervention reduces churn in that timing segment specifically

### Customer Health Score Framework

**Source:** Gainsight, Totango, and operator patterns across B2B SaaS companies
**When to use:** When you have 20+ customers and manual tracking is breaking down

**Steps to build a health score:**

1. **Select 4–6 signals** — Choose signals you can actually measure. Every signal should have a direction: higher = healthier or lower = healthier. Common signals:
   - Login/usage frequency (weekly active users as % of licensed seats)
   - Feature adoption depth (has the customer used X core features?)
   - Support ticket volume (high volume = not healthy)
   - NPS or CSAT score
   - Executive engagement (did they attend QBR? are they responsive?)
   - Billing health (are invoices paid on time?)

2. **Weight the signals** — Not all signals are equally predictive. Start with intuitive weights (based on your understanding of what predicts churn). Validate by checking whether health scores from 6 months ago predicted actual renewal/churn outcomes. Adjust weights accordingly.

3. **Define thresholds** — For each signal, define what "healthy," "warning," and "at-risk" looks like numerically. Example: Login frequency — Healthy: ≥ 70% of licensed seats active monthly; Warning: 40–69%; At-risk: < 40%.

4. **Calculate and publish** — Update health scores monthly (or weekly if feasible). Surface them in a CS dashboard that the whole team can see. Any customer with a score below 40 gets immediate outreach within 5 business days.

5. **Act on the score** — A health score is only valuable if it drives actions. Define what CS should do when a customer drops from green to yellow, and from yellow to red. Make the action automatic (if X happens, CS does Y within Z days).

```
│
├─ NO (health score 50–100) → Monitor. Review at next scheduled QBR.
│   └─ Has health score dropped > 15 points in 30 days?
│       ├─ YES → Investigate even if still green. Recent drops predict future churn.
│       └─ NO → No action required.
│
└─ YES (health score < 50) → How long have they been a customer?
    │
    ├─ < 60 days → Onboarding at-risk. Escalate to onboarding intervention.
    │   └─ Action: Book emergency call with champion AND IT contact within 48 hours.
    │              Goal: identify and remove the specific blocker to activation.
    │
    ├─ 60–180 days → Adoption failure. Escalate to adoption playbook.
    │   └─ Action: Book QBR with champion AND their manager. Focus on: what
    │              value were you expecting? What have you gotten so far?
    │              What would success look like at 6 months?
    │
    └─ 180+ days → ROI or strategic issue. Escalate to executive.
        └─ Action: Request executive sponsor call (CEO/founder if needed).
                   Come prepared with value delivered data ($, time saved, etc.).
                   Listen more than you talk — what's the real issue?
```

### Decision 2: Should We Do a QBR (Quarterly Business Review)?

```
START: Is this customer's ARR > $X (your strategic account threshold)?
│
├─ YES → QBR is mandatory every quarter.
│   └─ Can you get the executive sponsor (VP or above) in the room?
│       ├─ YES → Run the QBR with full agenda (value delivered, roadmap, next quarter goals)
│       └─ NO → Reschedule. A QBR without the executive is a status update, not a business review.
│                Maximum 2 reschedules before escalating to your own executive.
│
└─ NO (below strategic threshold) → Is customer's health score < 60?
    │
    ├─ YES → Run a targeted check-in (not a full QBR). Focus on: what's blocking them?
    │
    └─ NO → At-scale, use email-based check-ins for low-ARR healthy accounts.
             Reserve synchronous time for health issues and strategic accounts.
```

### Decision 3: Is This Customer Expansion-Ready?

```
START: Is customer health score > 70 for at least 3 consecutive months?
│
├─ NO → Not ready for expansion conversation.
│   └─ Focus on health improvement first. Expansion conversations from unhealthy
│       customers often accelerate churn.
│
└─ YES → Is the customer hitting usage limits or expressing a new use case?
    │
    ├─ YES → Clear expansion signal. Route to expansion playbook.
    │   └─ Who initiates: CS (if relationship-based) or AE (if volume-based)?
    │       ├─ ARR impact < $X → CS handles directly
    │       └─ ARR impact > $X → CS creates opportunity, AE closes
    │
    └─ NO → Create the expansion opportunity proactively.
        └─ Review: has the customer unlocked enough of the product?
            ├─ NO → Run feature adoption workshop. Expansion follows adoption.
            └─ YES → Schedule business review to surface new use cases and ROI achieved.
```

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Treating All Churn the Same

- **What it looks like:** Monthly report shows "5% churn." Team does a retrospective. "We need to improve onboarding." No distinction between customers who churned in week 3 vs. month 14.
- **Why founders do it:** Aggregate churn is easy to measure. Segmented churn analysis takes more effort and feels like post-mortem work.
- **Why it's harmful:** The interventions for early churn (0–60 days) are completely different from late churn (12+ months). Optimizing onboarding won't save a customer who churned because their VP-level champion left and nobody built an executive relationship with the new one.
- **What to do instead:** Segment every churned customer by when they churned (0–30, 31–180, 6–18 months, 18+ months) before asking why. The "when" tells you which intervention category to focus on.

### 2. Measuring NPS Without Acting on It

- **What it looks like:** Company sends NPS surveys quarterly, reports the score to the board ("NPS is 42, up from 38"), and moves on. Detractors don't receive outreach. Promoters aren't asked for referrals.
- **Why founders do it:** NPS measurement feels like CS work. Closing the loop takes time and is awkward (especially with detractors).
- **Why it's harmful:** NPS as a vanity metric is worse than not measuring it. Customers who give you a 3/10 and never hear from you are being told "we don't care." Promoters who give you a 10/10 and aren't asked for referrals represent unrealized growth.
- **What to do instead:** Build a closed-loop NPS process: detractors get a recovery call within 48 hours (not an email — a call); passives get a discovery call ("what would move you from a 7 to a 9?"); promoters get an ask ("would you be willing to intro us to one person who has this problem?").

### 3. Onboarding by Features, Not Outcomes

- **What it looks like:** Onboarding flow walks customers through every feature of the product: "Here's the dashboard. Here's the settings menu. Here's how to invite team members. Here's how to generate reports." Two-hour onboarding call. Customer is overwhelmed.
- **Why founders do it:** Founders know the product deeply and want customers to understand everything. Feature-based onboarding is how they would want to learn a new tool.
- **Why it's harmful:** Customers don't care about features — they care about outcomes. Feature-heavy onboarding creates cognitive overload and delays the moment the customer feels the value. Every hour between "signed" and "first value" is a churn risk hour.
- **What to do instead:** Design onboarding backward from TTFV. Ask: "What is the minimum number of steps a customer must complete to reach first value?" Build those steps — and ONLY those steps — into the onboarding flow. Every other feature can wait.

### 4. Health Score Without Action Criteria

- **What it looks like:** CS team builds a sophisticated health score (usage + support + NPS + billing). Dashboard is beautiful. Every customer has a score. When a score drops to red, CS team discusses it in the weekly meeting. No defined action protocol.
- **Why founders do it:** Building the score feels like the hard work. Defining action protocols seems obvious ("of course we'll do something when someone goes red").
- **Why it's harmful:** Without a defined action protocol, every CS rep makes a different decision. Some respond in 24 hours; others wait a week. Score drops aren't caught during high-volume periods. The health score becomes a measurement exercise, not a retention system.
- **What to do instead:** For every health threshold crossing (green → yellow, yellow → red), define exactly: who receives an alert, what action they take, and within what time window. "If score drops below 50: CS lead receives Slack alert; books call within 3 business days; logs outcome in CRM within 24 hours of call."

### 5. CS as Reactive Support

- **What it looks like:** CS team spends 80% of time responding to support tickets and inbound customer questions. Proactive outreach, QBRs, and health score reviews are "when we have time" activities.
- **Why founders do it:** Support is measurable and urgent. Proactive CS is important but not urgent. Reactive work fills the time.
- **Why it's harmful:** By the time a customer contacts support about a problem, the churn risk is already elevated. Proactive CS catches the problem before the customer even knows they have one. Teams that spend > 50% on reactive support are not doing customer success — they are doing customer support.
- **What to do instead:** Set a proactive-to-reactive ratio target (minimum 60% proactive; ideally 70%+). Track it weekly. If reactive work is crowding out proactive work, the answer is not to work harder — it's to improve the product (reduce the cause of support tickets) or add headcount.

### 6. Expansion Conversations at the Wrong Time

- **What it looks like:** CS rep identifies a customer who would benefit from the premium tier. Customer has had 3 support tickets in the last 30 days and health score is 45. CS rep pitches the upgrade anyway because the timing aligns with a promotion.
- **Why founders do it:** Revenue targets create pressure to expand. A logical need exists even if the timing is wrong.
- **Why it's harmful:** Expansion conversations with unhealthy customers accelerate churn. The customer interprets the upsell as indifference to their problems. "They're asking me to spend more money before fixing the problems I'm already experiencing." Churn likelihood increases.
- **What to do instead:** Expansion is only appropriate when health score is ≥ 70 for at least 3 consecutive months. When an expansion need is identified in an unhealthy account, resolve the health issue first — then open the expansion conversation.
