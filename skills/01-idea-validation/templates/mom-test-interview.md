# Mom Test Interview Script

> Use this script to extract honest signal from potential customers without pitching your idea. Based on Rob Fitzpatrick's "The Mom Test" methodology: ask only about their life, past behavior, and actual spending — never ask for opinions about your idea.

## Opening (2 minutes)

**Goal:** Establish a non-pitching frame. Make clear you're doing research, not selling.

**Script:**
> "Thanks for making time. I'm doing research on how [their role/domain] handles [broad problem area — e.g., 'vendor invoices' not 'our invoice tool']. I'm not selling anything — I'm trying to understand the problem better before building anything. I'll mostly ask you questions about how you work today. Is that okay?"

**Do NOT say:**
- "I'm building a tool that..." (introduces the solution too early)
- "I have an idea I'd love to get your feedback on" (turns them into an evaluator)
- "This will only take 5 minutes" (if you actually need 30 — creates a lie from minute one)

## Warm-Up: Context Questions (3 minutes)

**Goal:** Understand their role, workflow, and context before diving into the problem.

1. "Can you give me a quick overview of your role and what you're responsible for day-to-day?"

2. "In a typical [week/month], what are the 3 most time-consuming tasks in your job?"

   *[Listen for whether [your problem area] appears unprompted. If it does, that's a strong early signal. If it doesn't, note that — you may need to probe deeper or it may not be a top-3 pain.]*

3. "What's the most frustrating recurring part of your work?"

   *[Again — does [your problem] appear without prompting? If yes, weight it heavily.]*

## Problem Exploration (15 minutes)

**Goal:** Get them to describe the specific problem in their own words, from lived experience.

### Phase 1: Surface the problem

4. "Tell me about the last time [problem area] caused you a headache. What actually happened?"

   *[Ask for a specific story, not a general description. "It happens all the time" is not a story. Probe: "Tell me about the most recent specific time."]*

5. "How often does this happen? Once a week, once a month?"

   *[Frequency determines pain acuity. Daily = burning problem. Monthly = manageable. Quarterly = nice-to-have.]*

6. "When it does happen, what does a bad outcome look like? What's the worst case?"

   *[Severity determines willingness to pay. "It's annoying" vs. "it cost us $10K last quarter" are very different signals.]*

### Phase 2: Understand current behavior

7. "How do you handle this today? Walk me through exactly what you do when [the problem occurs]."

   *[This is the highest-value question. Workarounds reveal confirmed pain. "I have a spreadsheet where I manually..." is gold. "It just kind of works itself out" is a kill signal.]*

8. "What tools or processes do you use for this?"

   *[Listen for: cobbled-together solutions (Excel + manual + VA), prior attempts to solve with a tool, abandoned tools. Each signals pain intensity.]*

9. "Have you ever tried to find a better solution? What did you look for, and what did you find?"

   *[Search history reveals prior intent. "I've tried [Tool X] and [Tool Y] and neither works" is strong signal. "I've never really looked" is a weak signal.]*

### Phase 3: Probe on money and time

10. "Roughly how much time does this take you or your team per [week/month]?"

    *[Convert to cost: $[hourly rate] × [hours] = implicit value of solution. If this is < $100/mo, your price ceiling is very low unless there are 10,000 of them.]*

11. "Does your team spend any money on solving this today — tools, contractors, anything like that?"

    *[Current spend = proven willingness to pay. "We pay $X to [Tool Y]" tells you the market price ceiling and confirms someone is already paying to solve it.]*

---

## Commitment Testing (5 minutes)

**Goal:** Separate enthusiastic talkers from likely buyers. Never ask "would you use this?" — observe their actual behavior.

12. "If you had a solution to [problem as they described it], what would that unlock for you or your team?"

    *[Listen for tangible, specific outcomes vs. vague benefits. "We'd save 5 hours a week" vs. "it would be nice I guess."]*

13. "Is this something you're actively trying to solve right now, or more of a background annoyance?"

    *[Active = they have a budget, an owner, a timeline. Background = no urgency, no decision to buy soon.]*

**If you choose to mention your concept (last 5 minutes only):**
> "I'm exploring a tool that [one sentence description]. Based on what you've told me — does that sound relevant to the problem you described?"

*[Watch their face/tone more than their words. Listen for: "Oh interesting, how does it work?" vs. "Sure, that sounds useful" (polite dismissal).]*

14. "Would you be open to seeing an early prototype when we have something to show?"

    *[Yes = soft signal. "Can I give you my card?" or "Can I pay to be first in line?" = strong signal. "Sure, send me an email sometime" = noise.]*

---

## Closing (2 minutes)

15. "Is there anything else about [problem area] that you wish I'd asked about?"

    *[Often surfaces the real problem or a bigger problem adjacent to what you're exploring.]*

16. "Is there anyone else you work with who deals with this problem I should talk to?"

    *[Referrals are a signal in themselves. Willing referral = they found the conversation valuable. "Not really" = either the problem isn't acute enough to be social, or they're not convinced you're serious.]*

## Common Mistakes Reference

### Questions that seem good but aren't

| Question | Problem | Replace With |
|---|---|---|
| "Do you think this is a big problem?" | Asks for evaluation, not experience | "How did you handle it last time?" |
| "On a scale of 1–10, how painful is this?" | Numerical abstraction — people guess | "Is this something you deal with this week?" |
| "What features would you want?" | Asks customers to design your product | "What's the worst part of your current approach?" |
| "Would you pay $X/month?" | Hypothetical, anchors on your price | "What do you currently spend on this?" |
| "Is this something your company would buy?" | Vague buying authority question | "Who else is involved when you buy tools like this?" |

### Red flags during the interview

- Subject struggles to recall a specific time the problem affected them
- All problems described are in the past tense ("it used to be an issue")
- Subject says "I think people would want this" rather than "I want this"
- Subject describes the problem as "not that bad actually"
- Interview ends without any forward commitment and subject doesn't offer to share their contact
