# VC Decision Criteria

> How venture capital investors actually evaluate early-stage deals — based on post-mortems from successful and failed investments, published partner frameworks, and research on VC decision-making patterns. Use this to understand what's really being evaluated during a fundraising process.

---

## The Core VC Math

Understanding VC decision-making requires understanding the financial math that constrains every decision an investor makes.

### Power Law Returns

Venture capital returns follow an extreme power law: 80% of a fund's returns typically come from 1–2 investments in a 20–30 company portfolio. This shapes every evaluation:

- The investor is NOT asking "will this company succeed?" in an average sense
- The investor IS asking "could this company be the one that returns the fund?"
- A company that returns 3× is a successful business but a poor VC investment — it doesn't move the needle on a portfolio

**Implication for founders:** Every VC question is really asking "can this be a $1B+ company?" Investors who seem dismissive of your current traction aren't necessarily wrong about your company — they may simply see that it can't reach the scale required to matter to their portfolio.

### Return Math

**Example portfolio:**
- $100M fund
- 20 investments, average check size $5M (varies significantly)
- Fund fees and carry: fund needs to return $300M net to LPs to be considered a "good fund" (3× net)
- If 15 of 20 companies fail (common): The remaining 5 must return $300M collectively
- If 1 company is a $500M exit (investors own 20%): that single company returns $100M — already a meaningful part of fund returns

**What VCs look for:** Companies that have a plausible path to a $500M–$3B+ exit within 7–10 years. Below $100M exit: not meaningful. $100M–$300M: helpful but not a fund-returner. $1B+: potentially fund-returning.

## VC Evaluation Framework (Priority Order)

Research on VC decision patterns consistently shows this priority order at the earliest stages:

### Priority 1: Founder Quality (Most Weighted, Especially Pre-Traction)

What "founder quality" actually means in VC evaluation:

**Domain expertise:**
- Has the founder worked in or deeply experienced this problem for ≥ 2 years?
- Do customers treat the founder as credible? (Not just "smart" but specifically credible in THIS domain)
- Does the founder know something specific about this problem that others don't — an insight that makes the company non-obvious?

**Evidence of execution:**
- Not "has this founder been successful before?" (though prior exits help enormously)
- "Has this founder demonstrated an ability to do hard things under pressure?"
- Evidence includes: starting things from zero (previous companies, side projects, movements), shipping products, selling to customers, hiring people into roles before they could afford them

**Tenacity and adaptability:**
- The company will definitely hit a wall. Will this founder push through or give up?
- Tenacity evidence: past pivots that worked, businesses that almost failed but didn't, personal challenges the founder navigated
- Adaptability evidence: founders who have changed their mind based on evidence, not stubbornness

**Self-awareness:**
- Can the founder accurately describe their own weaknesses?
- Founders who say "I'm great at everything" are flagged. Founders who say "I'm not a great salesperson — that's why I hired [person] as VP of Sales in month 6" are credible
- Self-awareness predicts coachability — investors want to help, and founders who can't receive feedback can't improve

**Communication clarity:**
- Can the founder explain what they're building clearly to a smart generalist?
- Founders who need 5 minutes to explain the product are harder to champion internally at VC firms (partners need to pitch each other)

### Priority 2: Market Size (Must Meet Minimum Threshold)

**The threshold:** VC-backable market = TAM ≥ $1B. This is not because every market must be $1B from day one, but because:
- The company needs room to grow into the market without hitting a ceiling
- VCs need a credible exit path to an acquirer or IPO that requires scale

**What "market size" means in practice:**
- Bottom-up calculation preferred: (# of target customers) × (annual price per customer) = TAM
- Top-down market reports as corroboration, not primary evidence
- Emerging markets get more credit: "This is a $100M market today, but it's growing 40% annually and we expect it to be $1B by 2028" can be compelling with evidence

**The "secret ingredient" test (from Marc Andreessen):**
> "Every great company has a secret — something true that almost nobody else believes yet. What do you know about this market that others don't?"

This is related to market size but distinct: investors want to understand WHY this market is larger or more important than it appears on the surface. The best companies at their founding looked like small or weird markets (Airbnb renting air mattresses, Uber for black cars).

### Priority 3: Traction (The Most Honest Signal)

Traction answers the question investors can't answer themselves: "Does anyone actually want this?"

**What counts as compelling traction by stage:**

| Stage | Minimum traction that gets a meeting | Traction that creates urgency |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-seed | 10+ paying customers OR 3 strong LOIs OR 500+ waitlist with 20%+ conversion | $5K+ MRR OR 20+ paying customers with growing retention |
| Seed | $10–30K MRR OR 25+ paying customers | $30–100K MRR, 15%+ MoM growth, < 5% monthly churn |
| Series A | $500K–$1M ARR | $1–2M ARR, 20%+ growth QoQ, NRR > 110% |

**What investors analyze beyond the revenue number:**

1. **Growth rate > absolute revenue:** "$10K MRR growing 20% month-over-month" is more interesting than "$100K MRR growing 2% month-over-month"

2. **Retention quality:** Cohort data. Do customers from Month 1 still use the product in Month 6? A company that acquires customers and immediately loses them is in a Leaky Bucket — each dollar spent on acquisition is wasted

3. **Net Revenue Retention (NRR):** Expansion revenue as a sign of deepening value. NRR > 100% (customers spend more over time) is a signal investors weight heavily — it means the growth engine is partially self-funding

4. **Customer quality vs. count:** 5 Fortune 500 customers signals something different from 5 solo founders. Customer quality validates that the product works in serious enterprise contexts

5. **The "pull" signal:** Are customers finding you, or are you finding customers? Organic inbound (customers who found you without paid acquisition or personal outreach) is a strong early PMF signal

### Priority 4: Differentiation and Defensibility

**What VCs are actually evaluating here:**
- Not "is the product unique today?" (it can be copied)
- "Is there a reason this company will be MORE differentiated in 3–5 years than it is today?"

**Defensibility moats VCs care about:**

| Moat type | Description | Examples | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Network effects** | Product gets better as more users join | Marketplace liquidity, social networks, communication tools | Very strong |
| **Data advantage** | Company accumulates proprietary data that improves the product and is impossible for competitors to replicate | AI companies with unique training data, healthcare data assets | Strong |
| **Switching costs** | Cost (time, money, disruption) of switching away from product is high | ERP systems, banking, deeply embedded workflow tools | Strong |
| **Economies of scale** | Unit cost decreases significantly as volume grows; new entrants can't match | Manufacturing, distribution networks | Strong in physical; weaker in software |
| **Brand** | Brand preference exceeds functional differentiation | Consumer brands, luxury, enterprise reputation | Medium (hard to build, easy to erode) |
| **Patents / regulatory** | IP or compliance creates legal barrier | Pharma, biotech, fintech licenses | Strong in applicable industries |
| **Ecosystem lock-in** | Platform with integrations/plugins that competitors can't replicate | Salesforce, Shopify, Apple App Store | Strong but takes time to build |

---

### Priority 5: Business Model (Unit Economics and Scalability)

At pre-seed and seed: investors expect estimated unit economics with labeled assumptions. At Series A: investors expect real data.

**Metrics investors evaluate:**

| Metric | What it reveals | Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| **Gross margin** | Quality of the business (high margin = capital-efficient scaling) | SaaS: ≥ 70%. Services: ≥ 40% |
| **LTV:CAC** | Efficiency of customer acquisition | ≥ 3:1 required; 5:1+ preferred |
| **Payback period** | How long to recoup acquisition cost | < 18 months for venture; < 12 months ideal |
| **MRR growth rate** | Trajectory of the revenue engine | 15–20% MoM = strong; 5–10% = acceptable; < 5% = concerning |
| **NRR** | Whether existing customers expand or churn | > 100% = healthy; > 120% = strong |
| **Revenue per employee** | Capital efficiency of the team | Varies by stage; Series A target: $150–250K/employee |

## VC Red Flags (Common Reasons Deals Are Passed)

### Deal-killers (nearly always pass)

1. **Founder will not sell / extreme control demands:** Founders who refuse to grant any pro-rata, require unanimous approval for any investor action, or are unwilling to take outside input create governance risk that most VCs won't accept

2. **Market too small with no adjacent expansion:** A $50M market with no adjacent path to $500M is a lifestyle business, not a venture-scale company

3. **Direct conflict with portfolio company:** Most VCs have anti-portfolio-conflict clauses. If a better-funded portfolio company is solving the same problem, the investor won't fund a competitor

4. **Cap table problems:** Undocumented equity promises, messy convertible notes at conflicting terms, founder disputes over equity — all destroy investor confidence in governance

5. **Dishonesty about metrics:** A single discovered inconsistency between pitched numbers and actuals destroys the deal. Investors do reference checks and data verification

### Common objections (negotiable with better evidence)

1. **"Come back when you have more traction":** The most common soft no. Translation: "We're not convinced yet but we're not saying never." Ask: "What specific milestone would change your view?" A real answer means they're interested. "Just more traction" means polite pass.

2. **"The market is too crowded":** Translation: "We don't yet understand your differentiation." Respond with the competitive analysis slide — not to defend your position, but to show you understand the landscape deeply

3. **"We don't usually invest in this space":** Translation: "We don't have conviction and we'd need a lot of education." Sometimes solvable with a warm referral to an investor who has the thesis. Sometimes a genuine mismatch — not every investor will fund every sector

4. **"Tell me more about your team":** Often code for "I'm not sure this team can execute at the required scale." Answer by being specific: not "we have 40 years of combined experience" but "Sarah built and sold the ops tool at [Company] and I was the 10th employee at [Company] before the Series B"

## What Investors Are Actually Doing in a First Meeting

Understanding the process helps founders optimize for it:

**First 5 minutes:** Investor is deciding whether to pay attention or mentally check out. Your job: establish credibility and make them curious about one specific thing.

**Minutes 5–20:** Investor is evaluating the problem and market. They're asking: "Is this a real, large, painful problem?" Not "is the solution clever?"

**Minutes 20–40:** Investor is evaluating the founder and team. They're asking: "Why is this team the right one for this specific problem?"

**Minutes 40–50:** Investor is evaluating traction and business model. They're asking: "Is there evidence this is working?"

**Final 10 minutes:** The investor has usually made a preliminary decision. Q&A is either (a) diligence on things they already want to fund, or (b) discovery of reasons NOT to fund. Listen to the quality of questions — specific diligence questions are positive; vague philosophical questions are often stalling
