# Investor Outreach Template

> Structured outreach sequence with email templates, timing guidance, and CRM tracking. Follow this sequence to create professional, targeted outreach that respects investors' time and maximizes your response rate.

---

## Outreach Strategy Overview

**The core principle:** One email, one message, one ask. Every communication should be shorter than the investor expects and more specific than most founders send.

**Timing architecture:**
- All first-contact emails sent within a 48-hour window (to create simultaneous pipeline)
- Follow-up 1: 5 business days after initial email (if no response)
- Follow-up 2: 10 business days after initial email (if no response)
- No third follow-up — if no response after 2 follow-ups, mark as "non-responsive"

**Tier prioritization:**
- **Start with Tier 2** (strong match, no warm intro) — practice and refine on these
- **Move to Tier 1** after your first 5–10 meetings improve your pitch (Week 2)
- **Tier 3** fills pipeline gaps and provides useful meeting practice

## Meeting Scripts

### Opening a First Meeting (First 60 seconds)

> "Thanks for making time. We've got [X minutes] — I'd like to spend [15–20] on our story and the rest on your questions. [Company] helps [specific customer] [specific outcome]. We're at [traction line]. I'll walk you through the problem, why now, and what we're building. Stop me if you want to go deeper anywhere."

### Asking the Hard Question (Second meeting or after positive first meeting)

> "I want to be direct — we're targeting to close by [date]. I'd love your honest perspective: is this something you'd want to lead, participate in, or is it not a fit for your thesis right now? Any answer is helpful — it lets me prioritize our process."

### Handling "We want to see more traction"

> "I appreciate the honesty. Can I ask — specifically what traction milestone would change your calculus? If you said '$100K MRR' or '3 months of 15% growth,' I'd know exactly what to come back with. Without that, it's hard for me to know whether to keep you in the loop or move on."

### Handling "Your valuation is too high"

> "I'm open to that conversation. Can you tell me what valuation you think is appropriate and how you're thinking about it? I want to understand your model — and I'll share ours. If we're far apart, it's better to know now."

## Investor Reference Check

Before signing with any lead investor, call 3–5 of their portfolio company CEOs. These calls take 15 minutes each and are worth more than any due diligence document.

Questions to ask:

1. "What's [Investor's] style when things are going well?"
2. "What's their style when things are going badly?"
3. "Have they ever blocked a decision you wanted to make? What happened?"
4. "If you were raising again, would you take their check?"
5. "What would you tell yourself before taking their check that you know now?"

Red flags in answers:
- "They're hands-off" can mean uninvolved (bad) or respectful of operator autonomy (good) — probe which
- Hesitation before answering question 4
- Stories about board dynamics during hard periods where the investor prioritized their portfolio exposure over founder relationship
