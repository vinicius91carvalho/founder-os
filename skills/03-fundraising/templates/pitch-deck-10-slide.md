# 10-Slide Pitch Deck Template

> A structured template for a standard early-stage (pre-seed through Series A) pitch deck. Based on Sequoia Capital's pitch framework and YC's demo day format. Each slide has one purpose — do not add slides and do not merge topics.

---

## Deck Specs

**Target length:** 10 slides (11 if you add an appendix)
**Presentation time:** 10–20 minutes (leave time for Q&A)
**File format:** PDF for email; live deck for meetings
**Design principle:** One message per slide. If you need more than one sentence to explain a slide, the slide has too much on it.

---

## Before You Start: Narrative Arc

Your deck should tell this story in sequence:

> "Here is a painful problem that affects millions of people. Here is our solution. Here is proof that the market is large. Here is how we make money. Here is evidence it's working. Here is why this team. Here is why we win. Here is the financial path. Here is what we need from you."

Every slide advances this narrative. If a slide doesn't advance it, cut the slide.

## Slide 1: Title / Company

**Purpose:** Orient the investor. One glance should tell them what you do.

**Required elements:**
- Company name and logo
- One-line tagline: "[Company] helps [specific customer] [accomplish specific outcome]"
- Contact information (founder name, email)
- Website

**Tagline test:** Can someone who has never heard of you understand what you do from this one line — without reading the rest of the deck?

**Content to fill in:**

```
Company Name: [___________]

Tagline: [Company] [verb] [specific customer] [accomplish/prevent/reduce what?]
Example: "AcmePay automates vendor invoice reconciliation for ops teams at SaaS companies"

Founder: [Name], [Email]
Website: [URL]
```

**Anti-patterns:** Jargon ("the AI-powered platform for next-gen workflow optimization"), strategy-speak ("we are reimagining the future of B2B relationships"), questions that don't answer what you do ("What if your invoice process just worked?")

---

## Slide 2: Problem

**Purpose:** Make the investor feel the pain before you show them the solution. This is the most important slide. If they don't believe the problem is real and urgent, nothing else matters.

**Required elements:**
- The specific, painful problem — not a macro trend
- Evidence that it is widespread (a number) and serious (a cost or consequence)
- A human story or specific customer quote that makes it visceral

**Content to fill in:**

```
The Problem:
[One sentence stating the core problem in plain language — not your value proposition,
the customer's actual pain]

Who experiences it:
[Specific customer type — job title, company size, situation]
Example: "Ops managers at 10–100 person SaaS companies"

How widespread:
[Number of people/companies affected]
Example: "3.2 million US companies with 10–100 employees"

The cost (pick the most compelling):
☐ Time: "[X] hours per [week/month] wasted on [specific task]"
☐ Money: "$[X] spent annually on [workaround/manual process]"
☐ Consequence: "[X]% of [customer type] experience [specific negative outcome] because of this"

Customer quote (verbatim, from your interviews):
"[Quote that conveys the frustration in the customer's own words]"
Source: [Job title, company type — no identifying details without permission]
```

**Anti-patterns:** Starting with "Did you know the [industry] market is $X billion?" (lead with pain, not market), describing the problem in your language instead of the customer's language, making the problem sound like a nice-to-have

## Slide 3: Solution

**Purpose:** Show how you solve the problem. Show, don't tell. Investors should see your product doing something specific.

**Required elements:**
- What your product does (1–2 sentences)
- How it solves the specific problem from slide 2
- A screenshot, demo gif, or clear visual of the product in use
- The primary "aha moment" — the specific thing that makes customers say "I need this"

**Content to fill in:**

```
Solution Statement:
[One sentence: "We [verb] [outcome] by [mechanism]"]
Example: "AcmePay eliminates manual invoice reconciliation by connecting directly to vendor
portals and auto-matching invoices to POs in real time"

Key Benefit (translate the mechanism to the customer outcome):
"[Customer] goes from [painful before state] to [desirable after state]"
Example: "Ops managers go from 10 hours per month on invoice reconciliation to same-day
close with zero manual entry"

Visual: [Product screenshot / demo gif showing the key workflow]
Caption: "[What the user sees in this screenshot and why it matters]"

Key differentiator (one sentence — what you do that alternatives don't):
"Unlike [primary alternative], we [specific differentiating capability]"
```

**Anti-patterns:** A feature list instead of an outcome, text-heavy description with no visual, explaining the technical architecture instead of showing the user experience

## Slide 4: Market

**Purpose:** Demonstrate that the market is large enough to support a venture-scale business. Investors need to believe this can be a $1B+ company.

**Required elements:**
- TAM (Total Addressable Market) — the full potential market if you served every possible customer
- SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) — the portion you can realistically target now
- SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) — your realistic target for years 1–3
- Bottom-up calculation (not just a top-down market report citation)

**Content to fill in:**

```
TAM: $[___]B
Calculation: [# of potential customers globally] × [annual willingness to pay] = $[TAM]
Example: "5M ops roles at companies with 10–500 employees × $2,400/year = $12B"

SAM: $[___]B
Calculation: [# of customers in your serviceable geography/segment] × [your price] = $[SAM]
Example: "1.5M US companies with 10–100 employees, tech-forward sectors × $1,200/year = $1.8B"

SOM (3-year target): $[___]M
Calculation: [Realistic market share assumption] × SAM = $[SOM]
Example: "0.5% of SAM = $9M ARR — achievable in Year 3 based on current growth rate"

Market growth rate: [X]% CAGR
Source: [Market research or proxy data — cite it]

Key market trend driving urgency:
[What is changing in the market that makes this the right time?]
Example: "Remote work adoption → 40% increase in distributed team invoicing complexity"
```

**Anti-patterns:** Only citing top-down market reports without a bottom-up calculation ("the global AP software market is $3.4B per Gartner"), claiming a TAM larger than your addressable segment can support, no commentary on why NOW is the right time

## Slide 5: Business Model

**Purpose:** Explain how you make money — simply and specifically. Investors need to see a clear path from customer to revenue.

**Required elements:**
- Revenue model type (subscription, transaction, usage, etc.)
- Pricing structure
- Current or projected unit economics (LTV, CAC, payback — even if early estimates)
- Revenue progression (if you have it)

**Content to fill in:**

```
Revenue Model: [Subscription / Transaction fee / Usage-based / Other]

Pricing:
Tier 1 — [Name]: $[___]/month per [seat/company/usage unit]
Tier 2 — [Name]: $[___]/month per [seat/company/usage unit]
Tier 3 — [Name]: $[___]/month (or "Custom")

Target Customer for Primary Revenue Tier: [Who do most paying customers land on?]

Unit Economics (label as ACTUAL or ESTIMATE):
Average Contract Value (ACV): $[___]/year [ACTUAL/ESTIMATE]
Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC): $[___] [ACTUAL/ESTIMATE]
Gross Margin: [___]% [ACTUAL/ESTIMATE]
LTV:CAC: [___]:1 [ACTUAL/ESTIMATE]
Payback Period: [___] months [ACTUAL/ESTIMATE]

Current Revenue (if applicable): $[___] ARR / MRR
Revenue Growth Rate: [___]% [monthly/quarterly]
```

**Anti-patterns:** "We'll figure out monetization later," overly complex revenue diagrams, projections without unit economics, claiming margins before you have real cost data

## Slide 6: Traction

**Purpose:** Prove the hypothesis is working. This is the slide that converts skeptical investors. Hard evidence of traction is worth more than any narrative.

**Required elements (show the most compelling combination of what you have):**
- Revenue metrics (ARR, MRR, growth rate)
- Customer metrics (count, logo quality, NPS/CSAT, retention)
- Product metrics (DAU, engagement, North Star metric)
- Growth rate (the single most important number — investors invest in trajectories, not snapshots)

**Content to fill in:**

```
Primary Traction Metric: [The metric that best captures "this is working"]
Current value: [___]
MoM/QoQ growth rate: [___]%
Since [date]: [show the trajectory]

Revenue (if applicable):
Current ARR: $[___]
MoM growth: [___]%
Revenue chart: [Include a bar chart showing revenue growth over time — labeled by month]

Customers:
Total paying customers: [___]
Customer growth: [X customers in Month 1 → Y customers in Month N]
Notable customers (if permitted to disclose): [Company names or "Fortune 500 retailers," "Series A SaaS startups"]
Customer retention: [X]% annual retention [or monthly churn %]

Key milestones achieved:
☐ [Milestone 1 with date — e.g., "First $10K MRR — Month 7"]
☐ [Milestone 2 with date]
☐ [Milestone 3 with date]

Additional signals (if pre-revenue):
• Waitlist: [___] signups, [___]% conversion to active users
• Pilot customers: [___] companies in paid/sponsored pilot
• LOIs: [___] letters of intent with [total ARR value]
• Survey signal: [___]% of users say "very disappointed" if product disappeared
```

**Anti-patterns:** Using total registered users instead of active users, showing growth without context (3 customers → 5 customers is not compelling growth), omitting churn data (investors will ask)

## Slide 7: Team

**Purpose:** Answer the question: "Why are THESE people the right team for THIS problem?" Founder quality is the primary variable in early-stage investing.

**Required elements:**
- Names, roles, and photos of founders and key hires
- Domain expertise directly relevant to this problem
- Specific evidence of execution capability (prior companies, exits, key hires)
- Why this team, for this problem, right now

**Content to fill in:**

```
[Founder 1 Name] — [Title: CEO/CTO/CPO]
Domain Expertise: [Specific relevance to this problem — not generic credentials]
Example: "Led ops team at [Company] for 3 years — experienced this problem firsthand"
Prior: [2–3 most relevant experiences — NOT a full CV]
Education: [Optional — only if directly relevant, e.g., PhD in relevant field]

[Founder 2 Name] — [Title]
Domain Expertise: [Specific relevance]
Prior: [2–3 most relevant experiences]

[Key Hire / Advisor 1 Name] — [Role]
Why they matter: [One sentence on their specific contribution]

Advisor: [Name] — [Credibility signal]
Why they matter: [One sentence]

Team slide key question to answer:
"Why is THIS team — not a team of equally smart people — better positioned to solve this
specific problem?"
Answer: [3–5 sentences in your notes for the verbal narrative — not on the slide]
```

**Anti-patterns:** Generic bios ("Sarah is a passionate entrepreneur with 15 years of experience"), listing impressive company names without context on what you actually did there, photos but no evidence of relevant expertise

## Slide 8: Competition

**Purpose:** Show that you understand the competitive landscape and have a clear position where you win. Do NOT say "there is no competition" — that means there is no market.

**Required elements:**
- A 2×2 matrix or positioning map with axes you win on
- Named competitors (3–5) with honest assessment of where they're strong and where they fail
- Your specific differentiation — why customers choose you over alternatives

**Content to fill in:**

```
Primary competitors:
| Competitor | What they do well | Where they fall short | Your differentiation vs. them |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Competitor 1] | | | |
| [Competitor 2] | | | |
| [Competitor 3] | | | |
| Status quo (Excel/manual) | [Familiar, free, flexible] | [Slow, error-prone, doesn't scale] | |

2×2 Matrix:
X-axis: [Dimension where you win — e.g., "Automation Level"]
Y-axis: [Dimension where you win — e.g., "SMB Accessibility"]
Plot your competitors in the matrix. You should naturally be in the top-right.

NOTE: If all competitors cluster in bottom-left and you're perfectly top-right,
investors will be skeptical. Be honest — you may win on these two dimensions
but lose on price, feature breadth, or enterprise support. Acknowledge that.

Your defensible position (one sentence):
"We are the only [category] that [unique capability] for [specific customer segment]"
```

**Anti-patterns:** The "we have no competition" claim (signals market naivety), a 2×2 that looks too good to be true, not naming specific competitors, treating all competitors as identical

## Slide 9: Financials

**Purpose:** Show that you understand the business economics and have a credible plan. Do NOT try to prove you'll be profitable. Investors know 3-year projections are directional, not precise — they're evaluating whether your assumptions are reasonable.

**Required elements:**
- 3-year financial projections (Year 1 monthly, Year 2–3 annually)
- Key assumptions visible (not buried in a spreadsheet)
- Current burn rate and runway
- Headcount plan

**Content to fill in:**

```
Current State:
Monthly Burn Rate: $[___]
Current Runway: [___] months (at current burn)
Monthly Revenue Run Rate: $[___] MRR

Projections (conservative / base / optimistic):
| Metric | Year 1 End | Year 2 End | Year 3 End |
|---|---|---|---|
| ARR | $[___] | $[___] | $[___] |
| Gross Margin | [___]% | [___]% | [___]% |
| Headcount | [___] | [___] | [___] |
| Net Burn (monthly) | $[___] | $[___] | $[___] |

Key assumptions driving projections:
1. [Assumption 1 — e.g., "Monthly churn: 2%"]
2. [Assumption 2 — e.g., "CAC: $800 blended, declining to $500 by Month 18"]
3. [Assumption 3 — e.g., "Sales cycle: 30 days for SMB"]
4. [Assumption 4 — e.g., "New AEs ramp to quota in 90 days"]

Path to profitability (optional but useful):
At [ARR target], we reach gross profit breakeven. At [ARR target], we reach EBITDA profitability.
Timeline: [Month/Year]
```

**Anti-patterns:** Precision that implies false confidence ($4,237,500 ARR in Year 2), projections without visible assumptions, revenue hockey sticks without headcount to support them, ignoring burn and runway

## Slide 10: The Ask

**Purpose:** Tell the investor specifically what you need and what you'll do with it. Vagueness here signals unpreparedness.

**Required elements:**
- Raise amount (specific number)
- Instrument (SAFE, Series A equity, note)
- Valuation cap or pre-money valuation
- Use of funds (3–4 buckets with percentages)
- Milestone: what will this capital allow you to achieve?
- Existing investors / commitment to date (if any)

**Content to fill in:**

```
Raising: $[___] [Amount — specific]
Instrument: [Post-money SAFE / Priced Seed Round / Series A]
Valuation Cap / Pre-money: $[___]

Use of Funds:
[%] Engineering / Product — [what specifically: # of hires, what they'll build]
[%] Sales & Marketing — [what specifically: CAC budget, SDR hires, channel investment]
[%] Operations / Infrastructure — [what specifically]
[%] Runway / Working Capital — [how many months this provides]

Milestone this capital funds:
"This round funds us to [specific, measurable state] by [month/year], at which point
we are positioned to raise a [Series A / follow-on] at $[target valuation]"
Example: "We reach $1M ARR by Q4 2026, which positions us for a Series A at
a $15–20M pre-money valuation"

Current commitments (if any):
"[Investor name or type] has committed $[___]. We have [X weeks] to close."

Contact to proceed:
[Founder name], [email], [phone — optional]
```

**Anti-patterns:** "We're raising up to $[amount]" — be specific. "Flexible on terms" signals weakness. Not having a milestone for the capital signals you haven't done the financial planning
