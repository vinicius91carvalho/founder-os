# Financial Decision Criteria Framework

> **Purpose:** Clear, specific criteria for the most common financial decisions startup founders face: when to hire, when to cut, and when to raise. Replace gut feel with decision rules.

---

> **Financial Disclaimer:** The criteria in this framework are general guidelines based on startup best practices. They do not constitute financial advice. Your specific situation may differ materially. Consult a qualified accountant or financial advisor before making significant financial decisions.

*Note: This framework is educational. Consult a qualified accountant for advice specific to your situation.*

## Part 1: When to Hire

Hiring is the largest financial decision a startup makes. Each full-time hire typically costs 1.2–1.4× salary in total (salary + benefits + equipment + recruiting), and takes 3–6 months before they're fully productive.

### Decision Criteria: Should We Make This Hire?

Run through all five gates. A "no" at any gate means do not hire:

**Gate 1: Outcome Clarity**
- [ ] Can you write down what this person will have produced in 90 days — specifically? (Not "help with sales" but "close 5 new deals generating $25k MRR")
- [ ] If you can't answer this: NOT READY. Define the outcome first.

**Gate 2: Financial Capacity**
- [ ] After this hire, does your conservative-case runway still exceed 12 months?
  - Calculate: (Current cash) ÷ (Current net burn + incremental hire cost) = [X months]
  - If < 12 months in conservative case: DO NOT HIRE unless you have a committed round closing within 60 days
- [ ] Is this position in the approved headcount plan (i.e., budgeted)?

**Gate 3: Founder Time**
- [ ] Does the hiring manager have capacity to onboard this person for the first 30 days?
  - "Capacity" = can spend 2–3 hours/week focused on this person's development
  - If not: delay until they do. A mis-onboarded hire is as bad as a bad hire.

**Gate 4: Revenue Correlation**
- [ ] Is this hire directly tied to one of:
  - (a) Revenue generation (sales, demand gen, revenue CS)
  - (b) Revenue retention (product, engineering, CS)
  - (c) Essential operational capacity (you're actually breaking without it)
  - If none of the above: reconsider priority and timing.

**Gate 5: Alternative Options**
- [ ] Have you considered: contractor → fractional → part-time → full-time? Sometimes the answer is a 10-hour/week contractor, not a full-time hire.

### Hire Priority Framework

When you can only hire one of several needed roles, use this priority order:

| Priority | Role Type | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Revenue-generating (first sales hire) | Revenue solves all other problems |
| 2 | Revenue-retaining (product, engineering for core features) | Churn destroys what sales builds |
| 3 | Revenue-enabling (demand gen, data, CS at scale) | Multiplies revenue impact |
| 4 | Operational essentials (finance, legal, HR when breaking) | Hire when pain is acute, not preventive |
| 5 | Nice-to-have functions | Almost always premature at < 50 employees |

### When to Hire Finance Help Specifically

| Trigger | Resource | Estimated Cost |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Incorporated with first employees | DIY with QuickBooks | $50–150/month |
| > $10k MRR OR seed round raised | Bookkeeper | $300–800/month |
| > $50k MRR OR Series A prep (6 months out) | Fractional CFO | $3–8k/month |
| Series A closed, board reporting expected | VP Finance or CFO | $150–250k salary + 0.25–0.75% equity |
| Series B+, multiple products or regions | CFO with Big 4 or investment banking background | $250–450k+ salary + equity |

**Signs you need to upgrade finance sooner:**
- Monthly close takes > 5 business days consistently
- You've been surprised by an expense or revenue miss that the books should have flagged
- An investor or board member asked a financial question you couldn't answer
- You're spending > 5 hours/week on financial admin

## Part 2: When to Cut

Cost cutting has the same failure mode as hiring: too late, too little, or misdiagnosed (cutting the wrong things).

### Decision Criteria: Is a Cut Required?

**Mandatory cut signals (act immediately):**
- Conservative-case runway < 6 months AND no committed round
- Net burn has increased > 30% without proportional revenue growth over the same period
- Revenue growth has stalled 2+ consecutive months AND pipeline doesn't justify recovery
- A major customer churns and no replacement pipeline exists

**Watch signals (investigate, don't cut yet):**
- Conservative-case runway 6–9 months
- Net burn increased 15–30% MoM without clear driver
- LTV:CAC ratio dropping below 3:1

**No action required:**
- Conservative-case runway > 12 months
- Burn increase is planned (approved headcount, marketing investment) with expected revenue return

### Cut Sequencing (in order)

Cut in this sequence — earlier items preserve more team and morale:

| Order | Cut Type | Monthly Impact | Reversibility | Speed |
|-------|---------|---------------|--------------|-------|
| 1 | Pause all new spending (freeze open reqs, pause non-committed vendors) | $5–30k | High | Immediate |
| 2 | Cancel or renegotiate non-essential SaaS and subscriptions | $2–15k | High | 2–4 weeks |
| 3 | Reduce paid advertising to proven ROI-positive channels only | $5–50k | High | Immediate |
| 4 | Delay or eliminate non-essential travel and events | $2–10k | High | Immediate |
| 5 | Renegotiate major vendor contracts (office, cloud, legal) | $5–30k | Medium | 2–8 weeks |
| 6 | Reduce contractor/freelancer hours | $5–30k | Medium | 2 weeks |
| 7 | Executive salary deferrals (voluntary, document carefully) | $15–50k | Low | 2 weeks |
| 8 | Headcount reduction (layoffs) | $30–200k+ | Very Low | 4–8 weeks |

**Important:** Do not skip steps. Layoffs should be a last resort after exhausting all other options — the cost (severance, recruiting to rehire, morale impact) often makes them less financially beneficial than they appear.

### How to Evaluate a Cut Decision

For any proposed cut, answer:

1. **How much does this save per month?** (Monthly cash impact)
2. **What is the one-time cost to make the cut?** (Severance, contract termination fees, transition costs)
3. **Break-even period:** (One-time cost ÷ monthly savings = months to break even). If > 6 months, the cut may not be worth it unless you have a long runway crisis.
4. **What do we lose?** (Revenue risk, capability loss, morale impact)
5. **Is this reversible?** (Can we undo the cut if conditions improve?)

**Formula:**
```
Net benefit of cut = (Monthly savings × Remaining runway months) − One-time cost
```

If net benefit is positive: cut is likely worth it.
If net benefit is negative or close to zero: cut may cost more than it saves.

## Part 3: When to Raise

The most common mistake: raising too late (out of desperation) instead of too early (from strength).

### Optimal Fundraising Timing

**The fundraising window:**
- **Start process:** When conservative-case runway = 12–18 months
- **Target close:** When conservative-case runway = 9–12 months
- **Emergency raise:** When conservative-case runway < 6 months (you've lost significant leverage)
- **Never raise:** When runway < 3 months (bridge or shutdown territory)

**Why this math:** A seed or Series A process takes 3–6 months. If you start at 12 months, you have a cushion. If you start at 6 months, any delay in the process = a crisis.

### Decision Criteria: Is Now the Right Time to Raise?

**Favorable conditions (raise now):**
- [ ] You have 12–18 months of conservative-case runway (negotiating leverage)
- [ ] You have demonstrated PMF signals (NPS > 40, retention curve flattening, organic referrals)
- [ ] You have metrics that tell a clear, improving story (growth rate, retention, unit economics trending right)
- [ ] You know specifically what you'll do with the capital (specific milestones + use of funds = credibility)
- [ ] Market timing is favorable (your space is getting attention from investors)

**Unfavorable conditions (delay if possible):**
- [ ] Metrics are flat or declining — raise after a growth inflection
- [ ] Key metric stories are unclear (mixed signals on PMF)
- [ ] Macro environment is poor for funding (prices are compressed, timelines are longer)
- [ ] Core team is incomplete or recently disrupted

**Unfavorable but urgent (raise anyway):**
- [ ] Conservative-case runway < 9 months regardless of metrics or market

### How Much to Raise

**Principle:** Raise enough to hit the milestones that get you to the next round, plus a 20% buffer. Over-raising dilutes you. Under-raising forces a premature follow-on.

**Formula:**
```
Raise amount = (Target next round burn rate × 18–24 months) + 20% buffer
```

**Alternative check:**
- Identify the specific milestones that de-risk the next round (e.g., "get to $1M ARR with 80% gross margin")
- Build the plan to hit those milestones
- Total the cost of executing that plan
- Add 20–30% buffer for the unexpected
- That is your raise amount

**Round-specific benchmarks:**

| Round | Typical Raise | What It Should Fund |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| Pre-seed | $150k–$1M | Build MVP, get first 10 customers |
| Seed | $1M–$4M | Achieve PMF, get to $1M ARR |
| Series A | $5M–$20M | Scale proven model, build team, $3–10M ARR target |
| Series B | $15M–$50M | Expand markets, $10–30M ARR target |

### Instrument Selection

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity):**
- Best for: Pre-seed and seed before institutional lead
- Advantages: Fast (no negotiation on valuation), no interest, simple docs
- Key terms: Cap (valuation at which SAFE converts), Discount (10–20%), MFN clause
- Watch out for: Post-money SAFE vs. pre-money SAFE — post-money SAFEs are founder-unfavorable if multiple SAFEs are stacked without careful cap table modeling

**Convertible Note:**
- Best for: Bridge rounds between priced rounds
- Advantages: Simple, fast
- Disadvantages: Has interest rate and maturity date — creates pressure if priced round doesn't happen
- Key terms: Interest rate (4–8%), maturity (12–24 months), discount, cap

**Priced Round (Series A and beyond):**
- Best for: Rounds with institutional lead investors
- Involves: Negotiated pre-money valuation, term sheet, legal process (6–12 weeks)
- Key terms: Pre-money valuation, liquidation preference (1× non-participating is standard), pro-rata rights, anti-dilution

## Part 4: Financial Decision Quick-Reference

| Decision | Threshold | Action |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Conservative runway < 12 months | Rule | Start fundraise conversations immediately |
| Conservative runway < 6 months | Rule | Cut burn AND raise urgently in parallel |
| Conservative runway < 3 months | Rule | Bridge (existing investors), emergency raise, or shutdown planning |
| Net burn increased > 30% without revenue match | Signal | Investigate and present root cause to board |
| LTV:CAC < 2:1 | Signal | Pause CAC-increasing spend; fix retention or acquisition cost |
| LTV:CAC < 1:1 | Rule | Stop scaling customer acquisition — you're buying customers at a loss |
| Gross margin < 50% | Signal | Investigate COGS; SaaS target is 70–80%+ |
| Monthly close not happening | Signal | Hire bookkeeper now; you're flying blind |
| Burn increasing while revenue is flat | Signal | You're spending on growth before having growth — diagnose |
