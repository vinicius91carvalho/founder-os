## Diagnostic

Before giving advice, assess the founder's current financial maturity. Ask these questions to route to the right workflow:

### State Assessment

| State | Description | Route To |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Pre-Revenue** | No customers paying yet; living on founder savings or pre-seed; no structured accounting | Workflow A |
| **Early Revenue** | First revenue but no systematic tracking; books are informal or delayed; no monthly close | Workflow B |
| **Scaling Revenue** | $10k–$500k ARR; need proper accounting infrastructure, unit economics, and financial model | Workflow C |
| **Preparing for Next Round** | Raising Series A or beyond; need auditable financials, 3-year model, and CFO-level function | Workflow D |

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Do you know your exact monthly burn rate — gross (total cash out) AND net (after revenue)?
2. When did you last do a formal monthly close — reconciled bank statements, categorized all expenses, and produced a P&L?
3. Can you tell me your runway in months, and is that based on your current burn or your projected burn?
4. Do you have a 13-week cash flow forecast — a week-by-week view of cash in and cash out?

### Routing Logic

```
IF no revenue and no formal accounting → Route to Workflow A (Pre-Revenue)
ELIF has revenue but no systematic books or monthly close → Route to Workflow B (Early Revenue)
ELIF systematic books in place but no financial model → Route to Workflow C (Scaling Revenue)
ELSE (preparing to raise or has Series A+ investors) → Route to Workflow D (Next Round Prep)
```

## Workflows

### Workflow A: Pre-Revenue — Building the Foundation

**Objective:** Set up accounting infrastructure so you never have a gap in financial visibility.

**Steps:**

1. **Open a dedicated business bank account**
   - Action: Never co-mingle personal and business funds. Open a business checking account in the company's name. If you have an LLC or C-Corp, this is typically required. Recommended: Mercury, Brex, or a local business bank.
   - Output: Business bank account active and receiving all company payments
   - Decision point: If the company doesn't have a legal entity yet, open the account immediately after incorporating (see Skill 12)

2. **Choose accounting software**
   - Action: Set up QuickBooks Online (best for companies planning to raise) or Bench (if you want bookkeeping handled for you). Configure your Chart of Accounts with these standard categories: Revenue, COGS, R&D, Sales & Marketing, G&A, and Capital Expenditures.
   - Output: Accounting software configured with Chart of Accounts
   - Checkpoint: Can you run a basic P&L report? If yes, you're set up correctly.

3. **Track burn from Day 1**
   - Action: Every time money leaves the account, categorize it. At the end of each month, calculate: (1) Gross Burn = total cash out; (2) Net Burn = total cash out minus cash in from customers. Track in a simple spreadsheet or your accounting software.
   - Output: Monthly burn rate tracker
   - Next: Move to Workflow B when you sign your first paying customer

### Workflow B: Early Revenue — Establishing Financial Discipline

**Objective:** Build the financial visibility and hygiene that allows you to manage the business with data and communicate confidently with investors.

**Steps:**

1. **Implement monthly close**
   - Action: Within 5 business days of each month end, complete the close checklist (`templates/monthly-close.md`). This includes: bank reconciliation, categorizing all transactions, running P&L, updating burn and runway calculations, and filing in a persistent location.
   - Output: Monthly close completed and filed
   - Decision point: If close takes > 2 days and you're spending significant time on it, consider hiring a bookkeeper ($300–800/month) to handle reconciliation and categorization

2. **Calculate the right burn rate**
   - Action: Use `templates/burn-rate-calculator.md`. Understand the difference: **Gross Burn** = total cash out per month (all expenses, regardless of revenue). **Net Burn** = gross burn minus cash collected from customers. Both matter — gross burn shows your cost structure, net burn shows your true cash consumption rate.
   - Output: Gross and net burn calculated and tracked monthly
   - Checkpoint: Can you explain to an investor why your net burn is what it is, with a line-item breakdown? If not, your categorization is insufficient.

3. **Build a 13-week cash flow forecast**
   - Action: Use `templates/cash-flow-forecast.md`. Unlike the P&L (accrual), cash flow is on a cash basis — when money actually moves in and out of your account. This matters because: invoices don't equal cash, payroll has specific timing, and vendors may offer net-30/60 terms. A 13-week view catches cash crises 2–3 months before they happen.
   - Output: Rolling 13-week cash flow forecast updated weekly
   - Next: Move to Workflow C when MRR exceeds $10k/month or you raise a seed round

### Workflow C: Scaling Revenue — Financial Modeling and Unit Economics

**Objective:** Build the financial infrastructure and analytical capability to manage a growing business and raise the next round.

**Steps:**

1. **Build or update your financial model**
   - Action: A proper financial model has three interconnected sheets: (1) Revenue model (customers × ARPU, or expansion from existing cohorts); (2) P&L (revenue minus expenses); (3) Cash flow (P&L on cash basis). Add a Scenario tab (base, optimistic, pessimistic). Use `templates/runway-calculator.md` for the scenario structure.
   - Output: 3-statement financial model with scenarios
   - Decision point: If building the model takes > 2 weeks, hire a fractional CFO for a one-time engagement ($3–8k) to build it properly, then maintain it yourself

2. **Calculate and track unit economics**
   - Action: For every cohort of customers, track: LTV (lifetime value), CAC (customer acquisition cost), payback period (CAC / monthly gross margin), and LTV:CAC ratio. Target: LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1 for SaaS; payback period ≤ 12 months. If below these thresholds, investigate whether it's a pricing, churn, or acquisition cost issue.
   - Output: Unit economics dashboard updated monthly
   - Checkpoint: An investor should be able to ask "what's your LTV:CAC?" and receive an answer with methodology in under 2 minutes

3. **Upgrade accounting and finance function**
   - Action: At $20k+ MRR, a bookkeeper is mandatory. At $50k+ MRR or pre-Series A, engage a fractional CFO ($3–6k/month part-time) to own the model, investor reporting, and close process. At Series A close, begin planning for a full-time VP Finance / CFO hire.
   - Output: Finance function appropriately staffed for stage
   - Next: Move to Workflow D when Series A is 6 months out

### Workflow D: Next Round Prep — Audit-Ready Financials

**Objective:** Prepare financial infrastructure for Series A (and beyond) — where institutional investors require higher standards.

**Steps:**

1. **Prepare for financial due diligence**
   - Action: Institutional investors will request: 2+ years of historical financials (audited or reviewed), monthly MRR/ARR cohort data, unit economics by cohort and channel, 3-year financial model with assumptions documented, cap table (see Skill 12). Organize these into a data room 90 days before you start the raise.
   - Output: Series A data room financial section complete
   - Decision point: If you don't have an audited or reviewed financial statement, engage an accounting firm 6 months before your target close — audits take 2–4 months and cost $15–30k

2. **Engage a fractional or full-time CFO**
   - Action: By Series A, you need a senior finance person who can: own the model, manage the raise process, run board-level reporting, and build the finance function. Fractional CFO ($5–10k/month) bridges until you can afford full-time. Full-time VP Finance/CFO: typically $150–250k base + equity at Series A stage.
   - Output: Finance leadership in place
   - Checkpoint: Is your CFO (fractional or full-time) presenting the financials to the board and managing investor questions? If you're still doing this yourself, you haven't actually delegated.

3. **Implement financial controls**
   - Action: As company grows, implement: expense approval thresholds (employees can approve up to $X, manager up to $Y, CEO required above $Z); vendor payment review (AP should not also reconcile bank — segregation of duties); monthly board-level financial review; annual budget process.
   - Output: Financial controls documented and in practice
   - Next: Ongoing — repeat Workflow D for each subsequent round

## Frameworks

### Burn Rate and Runway Framework

**Source:** Standard VC/startup financial management practice
**When to use:** Monthly, and whenever making a significant financial decision

**The key distinction:**

| Metric | Formula | What It Tells You |
|--------|---------|------------------|
| **Gross Burn** | Total cash out per month | Cost structure; what you'd spend if you had zero revenue |
| **Net Burn** | Gross burn − revenue collected (cash basis) | True cash consumption rate; how fast you're spending the bank |
| **Runway (simple)** | Cash balance / net burn | Months until zero, assuming current burn rate |
| **Runway (realistic)** | Model future burn and revenue growth | More accurate, accounts for growth in both costs and revenue |

**The runway trap:** Many founders calculate runway on current net burn, but they're planning to hire aggressively. If you hire 5 people next quarter, your burn increases materially. Use `templates/runway-calculator.md` to model scenarios.

**Danger thresholds:**
- Runway < 12 months: Begin fundraising conversations immediately (raises take 3–6 months)
- Runway < 6 months: Fundraising is urgent; consider bridge options
- Runway < 3 months: Crisis mode — cut burn aggressively and activate emergency capital options

**Example application:**
> Company has $500k in the bank. Gross burn is $80k/month. Revenue collected is $20k/month. Net burn = $60k/month. Simple runway = $500k / $60k = 8.3 months. With planned hires, burn increases to $90k/month in 3 months. Realistic runway: approximately 7 months. Action: Start fundraising conversations now.

### 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast

**Source:** Standard corporate finance — adapted for startups
**When to use:** Weekly — this is not a "nice to have," it is a vital sign

**Why 13 weeks (not monthly):** Revenue arrives unevenly (enterprise contracts pay quarterly), payroll has specific timing (bi-weekly or semi-monthly), and a monthly view can miss a 3-week cash trough that causes a payroll crisis. 13 weeks is 90 days — the minimum lead time to address a cash problem.

**Steps:**
1. Start with your opening cash balance (confirmed bank balance, not accounting balance)
2. List all cash inflows by week: customer payments (use invoice due dates, not invoice dates), financing proceeds, other inflows
3. List all cash outflows by week: payroll (exact dates), rent, vendor payments (by due date), subscriptions, taxes
4. Sum: Opening balance + inflows − outflows = closing balance
5. Identify any week where closing balance drops below a safety threshold (suggest: 2× monthly payroll)
6. Take action 8–10 weeks before the projected trough — not when you're in it

**See `templates/cash-flow-forecast.md` for the full template.**

### When to Hire Finance Help

**Source:** Synthesized from Brex, a16z, and operator experience
**When to use:** When deciding whether to hire a bookkeeper, fractional CFO, or full-time finance leader

| Stage | Trigger | Finance Resource | Estimated Cost |
|-------|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| Pre-seed | Incorporated; first expenses | DIY with QuickBooks | $50–150/month software |
| Seed | > $10k MRR or seed round raised | Bookkeeper | $300–800/month |
| Post-seed | > $50k MRR or Series A prep starts | Fractional CFO | $3–8k/month |
| Series A | Round closed; board reporting expected | Full-time VP Finance or CFO | $150–250k + equity |
| Series B+ | Multi-product or multi-region | Full-time CFO (Big 4 background preferred) | $250–450k + equity |

**Signs you need to upgrade your finance function now:**
- Close takes > 5 business days
- You don't know your runway within 2 weeks of any given day
- An investor asked you a financial question and you couldn't answer in the meeting
- You've had a "surprise" expense that you didn't model

## Decision Trees

### Decision 1: Should I Cut Burn?

```
START: Is your runway less than 12 months?
│
├─ NO (12+ months) → Monitor. No immediate action required.
│   └─ Is runway trending DOWN due to rising costs (not planned investment)?
│       ├─ YES → Investigate root cause. Unplanned cost growth is a signal.
│       └─ NO → On track. Reforecast quarterly.
│
└─ YES (< 12 months) → Is a fundraise actively in progress?
    │
    ├─ YES → How confident are you in closing within 3 months?
    │   ├─ HIGH confidence → Maintain burn; accelerate close timeline.
    │   └─ LOW confidence → Start cutting NOW. Cuts take 2–3 months to impact burn.
    │                        Bridge financing is faster than a new round.
    │
    └─ NO → Start both: (1) Cut burn immediately, (2) Start fundraise conversations
        │
        └─ What are your largest expense categories?
            ├─ Headcount (typically 60–80% of burn)
            │   → Review roles: Is each person driving measurable revenue or retention?
            │     Pause backfills. Offer voluntary salary deferrals before layoffs.
            │
            └─ Vendor/software/infrastructure
                → Audit subscriptions. Renegotiate or cancel non-critical vendors.
                  Reduce cloud spend via committed-use discounts.
```

### Decision 2: Should I Raise Now or Wait?

```
START: What is your current runway?
│
├─ < 6 months → Raise immediately. You have no negotiating leverage with < 6 months.
│               Explore: bridge from existing investors, revenue-based financing,
│               venture debt. Do not wait.
│
├─ 6–12 months → Evaluate urgency:
│   └─ Are you at an inflection point (strong metrics, product-market fit signals)?
│       ├─ YES → Raise now. Raise when you can, not when you must.
│       └─ NO → Cut burn to extend to 18+ months, then raise from strength.
│
├─ 12–18 months → Optimal fundraising window:
│   └─ Do you have metrics that tell a clear story (growth, retention, unit economics)?
│       ├─ YES → Begin fundraise; process will take 3–6 months; you'll close with runway
│       └─ NO → Spend 2–3 months building the metrics story, then raise
│
└─ 18+ months → You have time. Raise strategically, not out of necessity.
    └─ Is there a strategic reason to raise now (M&A, market timing, specific investor)?
        ├─ YES → Opportunistic raise. Maintain leverage; don't need the capital urgently.
        └─ NO → Keep executing. Raise when metrics are better or terms are attractive.
```

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Confusing Gross and Net Burn

- **What it looks like:** Founder says "our burn rate is $50k/month" — but that's net burn after $30k in revenue. Gross burn is $80k. When describing the company to investors or making hiring decisions, they use the net number as if it represents the full cost structure.
- **Why founders do it:** Net burn is smaller and more reassuring. It feels like the right number to use.
- **Why it's harmful:** If revenue drops (a customer churns, a deal slips), gross burn becomes the actual burn instantly. Planning around net burn without understanding gross burn creates false confidence. Also: sophisticated investors will ask for gross burn; founders who don't know it look unsophisticated.
- **What to do instead:** Always track and communicate both. "Our gross burn is $80k, net burn is $50k. We'd need to cut to $X if revenue dropped significantly."

### 2. Calculating Runway on Current State, Not Future State

- **What it looks like:** Founder says "we have 14 months of runway." This is calculated on today's net burn. They've also committed to hiring 4 people next quarter. After those hires, runway is 8 months.
- **Why founders do it:** Current state is easy to calculate. Future state requires modeling.
- **Why it's harmful:** 14-month-runway decisions (like starting a 6-month raise process) are dangerous if actual runway is 8 months. Founders get caught mid-raise with a cash crisis.
- **What to do instead:** Calculate runway in three scenarios: (1) current burn stays flat; (2) planned hires and investments are made; (3) revenue growth is slower than planned. Use `templates/runway-calculator.md`. Make decisions based on scenario 2 or 3, not scenario 1.

### 3. Delaying the Monthly Close

- **What it looks like:** Books are updated quarterly (or "when there's time"). At any given moment, the founder doesn't know the real P&L or exact cash balance.
- **Why founders do it:** It's not urgent; other fires take priority. Bookkeeping feels administrative.
- **Why it's harmful:** You can't manage what you don't measure. Expense surprises happen. Revenue recognition errors compound. When you raise, investor due diligence takes twice as long because the books are a mess. Worst case: you find out you're out of cash in 45 days, not 90, and you had no warning.
- **What to do instead:** Close the books within 5 business days of every month end. If it takes longer than 2 hours for a sub-$100k MRR company, hire a bookkeeper.

### 4. Building a Fantasy Financial Model

- **What it looks like:** 3-year model showing hockey-stick revenue growth, expenses that grow linearly, and EBITDA positive by month 18. Assumptions page references "industry benchmarks" without citing sources.
- **Why founders do it:** Investors expect projections; optimism is natural; modeling is hard.
- **Why it's harmful:** Sophisticated investors can smell fantasy models. It signals that the founder doesn't understand their own business. More importantly, a model built on false assumptions doesn't help you manage the business.
- **What to do instead:** Build a model that you believe in. Ground assumptions in data you have (cohort retention, observed CAC, known payroll). Show your math. A conservative model with honest assumptions is more fundable than a fantasy model. Add scenarios so investors can stress-test.

### 5. Not Knowing the Unit Economics

- **What it looks like:** Founder can tell you total revenue and total marketing spend. Cannot tell you CAC, LTV, payback period, or LTV:CAC ratio. Cannot segment by channel (some channels may be deeply unprofitable).
- **Why founders do it:** Calculating unit economics requires careful attribution of revenue and costs — it's harder than looking at aggregate numbers.
- **Why it's harmful:** You can grow revenue while destroying value if you're acquiring customers at an LTV:CAC ratio below 1:1. You can invest in the wrong channels. Most importantly: every Series A investor asks about unit economics. Not knowing them is disqualifying.
- **What to do instead:** Calculate unit economics for your last 3 cohorts. If you don't have enough data, use the numbers you have and be explicit about the confidence level. Update monthly.

### 6. Treating Fundraising as a Financial Event, Not a Process

- **What it looks like:** Founder thinks "I have 6 months of runway, I'll go raise." Doesn't account for the 3–6 months the process takes, resulting in a runway crisis mid-raise.
- **Why founders do it:** Raising feels like the fix for financial problems. The timeline isn't intuitive.
- **Why it's harmful:** Founders raise at their lowest point of leverage (scared, runway short) instead of their highest (strong metrics, negotiating power). VCs can sense desperation. Worse: if the raise takes longer than expected, you're in a crisis.
- **What to do instead:** Start fundraising conversations 12 months before you need the money. Raise when you have 12–18 months of runway. Use the time when you have leverage — strong metrics, multiple term sheets, no urgency.
