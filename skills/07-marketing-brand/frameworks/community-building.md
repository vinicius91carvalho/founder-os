# Community Building Framework

> The community ladder: how to take people from passive consumers (lurkers) to active advocates (ambassadors). Each rung requires a specific activation trigger.

---

## Why Communities Work (and Why Most Fail)

**Why they work:**
- Communities create belonging, which is more powerful than features or price
- Members who are active in a community churn at 40–60% lower rates than non-members
- Community members generate more referrals, more content, and more honest feedback than any other customer segment
- A great community is a competitive moat — it can't be copied by feature parity

**Why most fail:**
- Founded as a marketing channel, not as a place of genuine value
- No one owns community management — it's "someone's part-time job"
- Content is 90% about the company's product, not about what members care about
- The value proposition is unclear: "join our community" is not a reason to join
- Launched too early, before there's critical mass (you need 30–50 engaged members before it feels alive)

## The Community Ladder

### Overview

```
AMBASSADOR (0.1%)
    ↑ [Give official role + co-create]
ADVOCATE (0.9%)
    ↑ [Recognize publicly + early access]
CONTRIBUTOR (9%)
    ↑ [Ask directly + lower the bar to post]
LURKER (90%)
    ↑ [Easy entry + valuable lurking experience]
AWARE (unknown)
    ↑ [Discovery — content marketing, word of mouth, distribution]
```

Each level transitions to the next through a specific trigger. The art of community building is knowing which trigger to apply to which person at which time.

---

## Level 1: Lurker → Contributor

**Defining the Lurker**

A lurker is someone who has joined your community but has never posted, commented, or directly engaged. This is 80–90% of most communities.

**Why lurkers are valuable (don't ignore them):**
- They are consuming content and forming impressions of your brand
- They are one good trigger away from becoming contributors
- Lurkers who never convert still benefit from the community — they may become customers

**Activation triggers:**

| Trigger | Why it works | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Direct ask by name** | Personalization breaks the social inertia of not posting | "@[name], curious what you think about [specific topic relevant to their background]" |
| **Low-stakes question** | Answering a question feels safer than posting an opinion | "Poll: which of these is your biggest challenge right now?" |
| **Welcome message from a human (not bot)** | Sets the norm that real people engage here | Manual DM within 24 hours of joining: "Hey [name], glad you're here. What are you working on?" |
| **Recognition of passive participation** | "I noticed you've been reading about [topic]" — makes lurking feel seen | Used sparingly; requires some tracking capability |
| **Easy contribution format** | Lower bar than a written post | "Drop your [tool stack / go-to resource / hot take] in the thread below" |

**Anti-triggers (what NOT to do):**
- Posting "introduce yourself!" threads without making them specific — they produce copy-paste responses that feel hollow
- Sending automated welcome sequences that feel like marketing
- Calling out lurkers publicly as "passive" — creates shame, not action

## Level 2: Contributor → Advocate

**Defining the Contributor**

A contributor has posted, commented, or directly engaged at least once. They've crossed the social inertia threshold.

**Why the Contributor level is the hardest to move through:**
- First contribution feels exposing — did it land well?
- If no one responds to their first post, they may never post again
- This is where most communities lose people

**Activation triggers:**

| Trigger | Why it works | Practical example |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| **Immediate, substantive response to first post** | Validates the risk they took; shows their voice matters | Respond within 2 hours; make it genuinely helpful or curious, not just "great post!" |
| **Public recognition** | Social proof that this person is valued | "Great point from [@name] yesterday about [topic]" in your weekly newsletter or community digest |
| **Ask for their expertise specifically** | Turns them from a poster into a resource | "I know you've dealt with [specific situation] — would you mind sharing how you handled it?" |
| **Invite to contribute to a project** | Elevates them from audience to co-creator | "We're putting together a resource on [topic]. Would you want to contribute your perspective?" |
| **Connect them to another member** | Makes them feel like a node in a network, not a spectator | "You should meet @[name] — you're both working on [similar problem]" |

**The 2-hour rule:**
Every first post in your community should receive a genuine response within 2 hours. This is non-negotiable. If you can't staff this, the community is too large for your moderation capacity or too small to be self-sustaining.

## Level 3: Advocate → Ambassador

**Defining the Advocate**

An advocate is a consistent contributor who also refers others to the community without being asked. They tell people "you should join this community" and share community content externally.

**Identifying advocates:**
- They've brought at least 1 new member to the community through direct referral
- They consistently provide high-quality, helpful responses (not just posting their own content)
- They defend community norms when someone is off-topic or rude
- They are among the first to respond to new posts

**Activation triggers for the → Ambassador step:**

| Trigger | Why it works | How to implement |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Official recognition with title/role** | Creates identity investment | "Community Moderator," "Featured Member," "Expert Contributor" — designate publicly |
| **Early access to product features or content** | Tangible benefit; signals trust | Beta program, early access newsletter, private briefings |
| **Co-creation opportunities** | Turns advocate into collaborator | Co-author a resource, co-host an AMA, be a guest on your podcast |
| **Exclusive access to founders/leadership** | Relationship with the people building the product | Monthly "insiders call" with the CEO; personal email introduction |
| **Public spotlight** | Feature their work, story, or expertise to the broader audience | Interview on your blog, feature in your newsletter, social media spotlight |

---

## Level 4: Ambassador

**Defining the Ambassador**

An ambassador actively recruits new members, enforces community standards without being asked, and publicly represents the community outside of it. They are your unpaid community team.

**Ambassador responsibilities (define explicitly):**
- Welcome new members and make sure no first post goes unresponded
- Flag off-topic or inappropriate content before you see it
- Refer 2–3 new high-quality members per month
- Represent the community at external events or in external channels

**Ambassador program structure:**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Selection criteria** | Clear, merit-based (contribution count, quality, tenure, referrals — not just "who likes us most") |
| **Benefits** | Tiered: recognition, early access, co-creation, direct founder relationship |
| **Expectations** | Written, specific, reasonable — 2–3 hours/month at most |
| **Offboarding** | Ambassador programs go stale; cycle in new ambassadors and gracefully retire long-tenured ones |

## Community Format Selection Guide

### Slack / Discord

**Best for:** Real-time conversation, technical communities, frequent engagement
**Required moderation:** 30+ minutes/day from a dedicated person
**Risk:** Low signal-to-noise if not actively managed; can feel chaotic as it grows
**Minimum viable size:** 50+ active members before it feels alive
**Tools:** Orbit.love (member analytics), Comeet (automated welcome), Common Room (multi-platform analytics)

### Newsletter Community

**Best for:** Written audiences, thought leaders, asynchronous engagement
**Required moderation:** Low — newsletter creation time + responding to replies
**Risk:** One-way communication; limited member-to-member interaction
**Minimum viable size:** 100+ subscribers before it feels like a community (most newsletters are audiences, not communities)
**Make it a community:** Feature member-submitted content, reader questions, member spotlights

### LinkedIn Group

**Best for:** B2B professional audiences; easier to grow than Slack
**Required moderation:** Low to medium
**Risk:** LinkedIn algorithm controls reach; group activity tends to be lower than Slack
**Minimum viable size:** 500+ members (groups feel empty at lower numbers)
**Make it work:** Post discussion questions 3× per week; approve new posts quickly

### In-Person Events / Meetups

**Best for:** High-trust, high-relationship depth; geographic or vertical communities
**Required moderation:** Event logistics
**Risk:** Hard to scale; expensive; geographic limitation
**Minimum viable size:** 20+ attendees for regular events to feel substantive
**Make it work:** Combine with online community to maintain engagement between events

## Community Metrics

Track these monthly:

| Metric | Definition | Healthy range |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Monthly Active Members (MAM) | Members who posted or commented in the last 30 days | > 10% of total members |
| D-30 Retention | % of new members still active 30 days after joining | > 40% |
| Contributor Rate | % of members who have ever posted | > 15% |
| Advocate Rate | % of members who have referred ≥ 1 new member | > 2% |
| Content Quality Score | Subjective — track number of "great posts" per week | Increasing trend |
| Time to First Response | Median time for a new post to get a response | < 2 hours |

**The danger signals:**
- MAM dropping month-over-month → engagement crisis; investigate root cause before adding members
- Time to first response > 4 hours → moderation gap; recruit ambassadors immediately
- 90%+ of posts from the same 5 people → community is a blog with comments, not a community

## Community Launch Playbook

### Pre-Launch (4–6 weeks before opening)

- [ ] Define community value proposition (1 sentence — what members get that they can't get elsewhere)
- [ ] Choose platform and set up structure (channels/categories)
- [ ] Identify 15–20 "founding members" — existing customers, fans, or friends who will seed activity
- [ ] Brief founding members: "We need you to post first for 4–6 weeks so it doesn't feel empty"
- [ ] Write 10 starter discussion posts to publish in the first week
- [ ] Set up welcome automation (if platform supports it) with personalization hook

### Launch Week

- [ ] Invite founding members first (soft launch — no public announcement)
- [ ] Post first discussion and get founding members to engage
- [ ] DM every new member personally within 24 hours of joining
- [ ] Post 2–3 times yourself this week

### First 30 Days

- [ ] Weekly digest of best content (establishes the norm of what "good" looks like)
- [ ] Welcome new members publicly by name in a weekly post
- [ ] Identify your first 3 natural advocates (they'll emerge in week 2–3)
- [ ] Reach 50 active members before inviting a broader audience

### Months 2–6

- [ ] Formalize ambassador program as community reaches 200+ members
- [ ] Launch monthly AMA, workshop, or expert session
- [ ] Begin tracking metrics; set monthly targets
- [ ] Run your first community-sourced project (content, research, resource library)
