# Skill 07: Marketing & Brand

> Build a brand that earns trust before it asks for attention — then distribute that brand relentlessly. The 80/20 rule of content: 20% creating, 80% distributing.

---

## Diagnostic

Before giving advice, assess the founder's current marketing and brand state. Ask these questions to route to the right workflow:

### State Assessment

| State | Description | Route To |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Pre-Brand** | No defined brand identity, voice, or positioning; founder is marketing by instinct | Workflow A |
| **Brand Definition** | Has product positioning but no consistent voice or visual identity; every piece of content sounds different | Workflow B |
| **Brand Creation** | Has a defined brand voice but no content engine; creating content sporadically | Workflow C |
| **Distribution** | Has content being produced; needs distribution and amplification strategy | Workflow D |
| **Community** | Distributing consistently; ready to build owned audience and community | Workflow E |

### Diagnostic Questions

1. Can you describe your brand in 3 words and give an example of a content piece that embodies each word?
2. If a new team member wrote a blog post for your company, would it sound like you — or like any generic SaaS company?
3. What percentage of your content effort is spent creating vs. distributing and repurposing?
4. Do you have an owned audience (email list, newsletter subscribers, community members) that is independent of any platform algorithm?

### Routing Logic

```
IF no defined brand voice or positioning → Route to Workflow A (Pre-Brand Foundation)
ELIF brand defined but not documented or consistent → Route to Workflow B (Brand Definition)
ELIF brand documented but content is sporadic or low → Route to Workflow C (Content Engine)
ELIF content being produced but not distributed systematically → Route to Workflow D (Distribution)
ELSE (content + distribution working; want to build community) → Route to Workflow E (Community Building)
```

## Workflows

### Workflow A: Pre-Brand Foundation — Positioning Before Personality

**Objective:** Establish the positioning foundation that everything else is built on. Brand voice without positioning is style without substance.

**Steps:**

1. **Define positioning before brand**
   - Action: Answer the April Dunford positioning questions (from Skill 02): Who is the target customer? What is the market category? What are the differentiators? What are the alternative comparisons? What is the unique value?
   - Output: Positioning statement (1 paragraph)
   - Decision point: If you can't complete positioning without guessing, run Skill 01 (Idea Validation) first

2. **Identify your brand archetype**
   - Action: Choose one of 3 startup brand archetypes that fits your positioning: Expert/Authority (you teach), Challenger (you fight the status quo), or Friend/Guide (you simplify). Every content decision flows from this.
   - Output: Brand archetype selection with rationale
   - Checkpoint: Look at your 3 best competitors. What archetype are they? Don't pick the same one if you can avoid it.

3. **Write the brand voice guide**
   - Action: Use `templates/brand-voice-guide.md` to define 3–4 voice attributes. For each: what it means in practice, what it sounds like, and what it does NOT sound like.
   - Output: Brand voice guide (starter version)
   - Next: Move to Workflow B to socialize and enforce the guide

### Workflow B: Brand Definition — Making It Consistent

**Objective:** Turn brand voice from a document into a lived practice across all content and communications.

**Steps:**

1. **Create the brand content test**
   - Action: Write 3 example sentences — one that is clearly in your brand voice, one that sounds generic, one that sounds like a competitor. Anyone creating content for you should be able to pass this test.
   - Output: Brand voice litmus test (3 example sentences)
   - Decision point: If team members can't tell the difference, the guide needs more examples

2. **Audit existing content**
   - Action: Review your last 10 pieces of public content (emails, posts, website copy). Score each: On-brand / Off-brand / Mixed. Identify the most common off-brand patterns.
   - Output: Content audit summary (top 3 off-brand patterns)
   - Checkpoint: If > 50% is off-brand, rewrite brand guide with more examples before creating new content

3. **Define visual identity markers**
   - Action: Even without a designer, define: primary color (hex), font choice (Google Fonts if needed), and image style (photography vs. illustration; formal vs. casual). These decisions prevent the random-color-every-post look.
   - Output: Visual identity reference (3 decisions)
   - Next: Move to Workflow C when voice and visual identity are consistent

### Workflow C: Content Engine — Producing Systematically

**Objective:** Build a sustainable content production system using the Pillar Content model.

**Steps:**

1. **Choose one content channel to own**
   - Action: Pick ONE channel to be excellent at before diversifying. Options by founder type: Long-form writing → Blog/Substack/LinkedIn newsletter. Verbal → Podcast. Visual → LinkedIn or Twitter. Video → YouTube.
   - Output: Primary content channel selected
   - Decision point: Choose based on your natural strength, not what you think you "should" do. You will quit what you hate.

2. **Build the pillar content framework**
   - Action: Each month, produce ONE cornerstone piece of content (3,000–5,000 words or 30+ min video). Derive all other content from this cornerstone. See Frameworks for the derivative content model.
   - Output: Monthly content calendar with 1 cornerstone + 8–12 derivative pieces
   - Checkpoint: Every derivative piece links back to the cornerstone and can stand alone

3. **Apply the 80/20 distribution rule**
   - Action: For every hour spent creating content, plan to spend 4 hours distributing it. Identify 5–8 distribution channels per piece before you write it.
   - Output: Content distribution checklist per piece
   - Next: Move to Workflow D when producing consistently (at least 1 cornerstone/month for 3 months)

### Workflow D: Distribution — Reach More People With Less New Content

**Objective:** Systematize distribution so every piece of content reaches its full audience, not just the first platform you post it on.

**Steps:**

1. **Build the distribution checklist**
   - Action: Use `frameworks/distribution-first.md` to create a repeatable checklist. For each piece of content, identify: which communities to share in, which newsletters to pitch, which accounts to tag, which email list to include it in.
   - Output: Distribution checklist (10–15 specific channels per content piece)
   - Decision point: Distribution channels should include at least one owned channel (email list) — platform-dependent channels can disappear

2. **Set up the SEO foundation**
   - Action: Identify 5–10 keywords your ICP searches for when they have the problem you solve. Write one long-form piece per keyword. Track rankings monthly.
   - Output: SEO keyword list with content plan
   - Checkpoint: Use free tools (Google Search Console, Ahrefs free tier) to verify search volume before writing

3. **Build the PR outreach machine**
   - Action: Use `templates/pr-outreach.md` to prepare a media pitch and journalist target list. Target: 3–5 journalists or newsletters per quarter. Goal: 1 placement per quarter.
   - Output: PR target list and pitch template
   - Next: Move to Workflow E when distribution generates inbound interest and audience growth

### Workflow E: Community Building — Own Your Audience

**Objective:** Convert individual followers into an engaged community that advocates for your brand without you asking.

**Steps:**

1. **Choose a community format**
   - Action: Select the format that matches your content style and audience behavior: Slack/Discord (high engagement, high management), Newsletter (owned, scalable), In-person events (high trust, low scale), LinkedIn Group (lower engagement, but easy to start).
   - Output: Community format selection with rationale
   - Decision point: Do not launch a Slack/Discord unless you have 30 minutes/day to moderate

2. **Define the value proposition for members**
   - Action: Write in one sentence why someone should join your community. NOT "to learn about [your product]." The community value must be independent of your product.
   - Output: Community value proposition (1 sentence)
   - Checkpoint: Would someone join even if they never become a customer?

3. **Build the member progression ladder**
   - Action: Use `frameworks/community-building.md` to define the journey from Lurker → Contributor → Advocate → Ambassador. Define the action that moves someone to each next level.
   - Output: Community ladder with activation triggers per level
   - Next: Measure monthly active members and % who reach each level. Feed community insights into Skill 05 (Product) and Skill 06 (Sales).

## Frameworks

### The 80/20 Distribution Principle

**Source:** Synthesized from Nathan Barry (ConvertKit), Gary Vaynerchuk, Rand Fishkin
**When to use:** Every time you create a piece of content

The single most common content marketing mistake: spending 80% of time creating and 20% distributing. The correct ratio is the inverse.

**Why the inversion works:**
- Your best content decays without distribution; good distribution + mediocre content often outperforms great content with no distribution
- Existing content can be repurposed to new audiences and new contexts without creating from scratch
- Distribution compounds: building relationships with newsletters, journalists, and communities multiplies your reach without increasing creation cost

**The 80/20 checklist per content piece:**

| Distribution Action | Time Cost | Reach Multiplier |
|--------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Post on primary channel | Low | 1× |
| Send to email list | Low | High (owned audience) |
| Share in 3–5 relevant communities (with context, not spam) | Medium | High |
| Tag 3 people mentioned or relevant | Low | Medium |
| Pitch to 2 newsletters for inclusion | Low | High |
| Create derivative social posts (3–5) | Low | Medium |
| Repurpose to secondary channel (thread, short video, podcast clip) | Medium | Medium |
| Update and republish after 6 months | Low | Medium (SEO) |

### Pillar Content Model

**Source:** Synthesized from HubSpot, Ann Handley, Content Marketing Institute
**When to use:** Planning your monthly content calendar

**Architecture:**

```
CORNERSTONE (1 per month)
    │
    ├─ One comprehensive, definitive piece on a topic your ICP cares about
    │   Examples: "The Complete Guide to [Topic]", "How We Did X (with data)"
    │   Format: Long-form post, comprehensive video, detailed guide
    │
    ├─ DERIVATIVE CONTENT (6–10 per cornerstone)
    │   ├─ Social thread (Twitter/X): 5–8 key points from the cornerstone
    │   ├─ LinkedIn post: One counterintuitive insight from the cornerstone
    │   ├─ Short video: 90-second talking-head summary of the key insight
    │   ├─ Quote card: One striking data point or quote, designed
    │   ├─ Newsletter edition: The cornerstone's key takeaways for your list
    │   ├─ Podcast segment: Interview angle or solo commentary on the theme
    │   ├─ Email sequence: 3-part sequence teaching the concept in depth
    │   └─ Community post: A question or discussion prompt based on the topic
    │
    └─ DISTRIBUTION (8–15 channels per cornerstone)
        ├─ Owned: email list, newsletter
        ├─ Earned: journalist pitch, newsletter partner, community mention
        └─ Borrowed: social media, republication on Medium/Dev.to
```

**Rule:** Write the cornerstone ONCE. Everything else is packaging.

### Community Building Ladder

**Source:** Synthesized from David Spinks (CMX), Rosie Sherry, Gartner
**When to use:** Designing and measuring community growth

**The Four Levels:**

| Level | Behavior | Percentage | Your Action |
|-------|---------|-----------|------------|
| **Lurker** | Reads content, never posts | 90% | Make content worth lurking. Easy entry (no barriers to join). Welcome DM or email. |
| **Contributor** | Replies, comments, answers questions | 9% | Give them a reason to contribute. Ask questions. Feature their contributions. |
| **Advocate** | Voluntarily promotes community to others | 0.9% | Recognize publicly. Give early access. Include in decisions. |
| **Ambassador** | Actively recruits new members and defends community standards | 0.1% | Give official role and title. Co-create content. Invite to exclusive events. |

**Activation rules:**
- The biggest error: trying to convert lurkers to ambassadors in one step
- One step at a time: get lurkers to comment once → get commenter to post their own insight → invite poster to help you with a community project
- The fastest activation: ask a lurker a question directly, by name

```
START: What is the purpose of this content?
│
├─ Drive SEO / organic search →
│   └─ Is there search volume for this keyword? (use Google Keyword Planner)
│       ├─ YES (>1K searches/month) → High value, create
│       └─ NO → Skip unless it serves another purpose
│
├─ Build brand / thought leadership →
│   └─ Does this content demonstrate a unique, defensible point of view?
│       ├─ YES → High value, create
│       └─ NO → Generic content → revise or skip
│
├─ Convert leads →
│   └─ Is this content requested by or relevant to your ICP?
│       ├─ YES → Create (bottom-funnel)
│       └─ NO → Top-of-funnel awareness, okay if brand-building
│
└─ Community engagement →
    └─ Will this spark conversation or provide value to members?
        ├─ YES → Post it
        └─ NO → Don't post filler — it dilutes community quality
```

### Decision 3: Should I Pursue PR?

```
START: Do you have a story worth covering?
│
├─ What do you have? →
│   ├─ Funding announcement → Tier A story (tech press)
│   ├─ Milestone / traction data → Tier B story (trade press, local)
│   ├─ Unique data or research → Tier A story (if the data is genuinely novel)
│   ├─ Customer success story → Tier C story (your owned channels; journalist profiles it if it's extraordinary)
│   └─ "We exist" / product launch with no traction → Not yet a story
│
└─ Is this the right time for PR?
    ├─ Pre-product-market fit → No. PR without retention is wasted goodwill.
    └─ Post-PMF, growth stage → Yes. PR can accelerate a flywheel that's already turning.
```

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Creating Without Distributing

- **What it looks like:** Publishing 3 blog posts per week that each get 15–30 views. No email list. No community. No distribution beyond auto-posting to Twitter.
- **Why founders do it:** Writing feels productive; distribution feels like self-promotion.
- **Why it's harmful:** Content that isn't read has zero marketing value. You burn creative energy on work that compounds nothing.
- **What to do instead:** Before writing your next piece, write your distribution plan. If you can't list 10 places this will go, don't write it yet.

### 2. Brand Voice Defined, Never Enforced

- **What it looks like:** "We are authentic, human, and bold" in a brand guide. Every social post sounds like a corporate press release. Every email says "We're excited to announce..."
- **Why founders do it:** Brand guidelines get written and forgotten. Inconsistency creeps in as the team grows.
- **Why it's harmful:** Brand trust requires consistency. Inconsistent brands feel untrustworthy, even if the product is excellent.
- **What to do instead:** The brand guide must have examples — one on-brand sentence, one off-brand sentence per attribute. Anyone writing for your brand passes the examples test before publishing.

### 3. Optimizing for Vanity Metrics

- **What it looks like:** Celebrating 10K Twitter followers while email open rate is 8%. Measuring success by impressions and likes, not by email signups, inbound leads, or referrals.
- **Why founders do it:** Follower counts and likes are visible and feel like social proof; conversions are harder to track.
- **Why it's harmful:** Vanity metrics don't pay salaries. Optimizing for reach without conversion is expensive PR with no commercial outcome.
- **What to do instead:** The only marketing metrics that matter: email subscribers (owned), inbound leads generated, and MRR attribution. Optimize for these, not for likes.

### 4. Trying to Win Every Channel Simultaneously

- **What it looks like:** Posting daily on LinkedIn, Twitter, Instagram, TikTok, and running a blog, podcast, and newsletter all at once. Everything is mediocre. Nothing compounds.
- **Why founders do it:** FOMO — every channel has a success story; the fear of missing the right one.
- **Why it's harmful:** Excellent content on one channel consistently compounds. Mediocre content on six channels produces nothing. Channel diversity before channel mastery is a trap.
- **What to do instead:** Own one channel for 6 months before adding a second. Being excellent on LinkedIn is worth more than being average on four platforms.

### 5. Building a Community as a Marketing Channel

- **What it looks like:** Founder creates a Slack community, then sends product announcements, promo codes, and sales content as the primary posts. Members leave.
- **Why founders do it:** Confuses "community" with "captive audience."
- **Why it's harmful:** Communities die when members sense they're being sold to. The goodwill invested in building the community is destroyed in weeks.
- **What to do instead:** Community value must be independent of your product. The community should answer: "What would members miss if the company shut down?" If the answer is "nothing," the community has no foundation.

### 6. PR Too Early (Premature Amplification)

- **What it looks like:** Founder gets TechCrunch coverage at launch. Traffic spikes. Product doesn't retain users. Spike fades in 2 days. No lasting benefit.
- **Why founders do it:** PR feels validating; investors like it; founders think it will fill the top of funnel.
- **Why it's harmful:** PR without retention is throwing gasoline on a leaky bucket. You burn your one shot at a major publication on a product that isn't ready.
- **What to do instead:** Get PR coverage AFTER you have product-market fit and retention. A TechCrunch story with a product that retains users can compound for years.

### 7. Copying Competitor Content Strategy

- **What it looks like:** Competitor has a popular podcast. You start a podcast. Competitor dominates YouTube. You start a YouTube channel. You're always 12 months behind in a format you don't enjoy.
- **Why founders do it:** Copying feels safer than inventing; there's proof of demand.
- **Why it's harmful:** You're competing at a disadvantage: less experience in the format, smaller existing audience, and probably lower motivation (you're copying, not creating).
- **What to do instead:** Find the format your competitor ISN'T using. If they dominate written content, go audio or video. If they're doing polished video, go raw/authentic. The uncontested channel is worth more than the crowded channel.
