## How to Use This Canvas

Work through the 5 components in order. Each step narrows the focus from "who competes with us" to "who we are the obvious choice for." Do not skip ahead or do the components out of order — the sequence is deliberate.

## Component 2: Unique Attributes

**Question to answer:** What do you have or do that the alternatives can't or won't?

Focus only on structural advantages — things that are either technically impossible for alternatives to replicate quickly, or things they won't do because of business model constraints. Feature-level differences ("better UI") are NOT unique attributes — a competitor can copy a feature in 6 months.

**Examples of genuine unique attributes:**
- "We own proprietary data they can't access" (data advantage)
- "Our API integrates with [specific ecosystem] they don't support" (integration lock-in)
- "We're built mobile-first — their product was designed for desktop and can't easily adapt" (architectural advantage)
- "We're 80% cheaper because we don't have a field sales team" (cost structure advantage)
- "Our founders spent 10 years in this industry and built the core algorithm based on that experience" (expertise moat)

**Question to answer:** Given your unique value themes, which specific customers benefit most — and value it most?

Not all customers benefit equally from your positioning. The best-fit customer is the one for whom your unique value themes matter most. These are the customers who will:
- Close fastest (because your differentiation is obvious to them)

## Component 5: Market Category

**Question to answer:** What category should you compete in — and why?

Your market category determines:
- Who you get compared to (define it wrong and you're compared to the wrong competitors)
- What customers already know about the category (existing belief systems work for or against you)
- What price benchmarks customers have in mind

**Three category options:**

| Option | Strategy | Risk | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Existing category** (e.g., "project management software") | Leverage existing demand; compete on differentiation | Must explain why you're different from established players | Best when category is well-understood, growing, and has identified pain |
| **Resegmented category** (e.g., "project management for construction teams") | Narrow the category to your best-fit customer | Smaller initial market but lower competition | Best when your unique attributes only matter to a specific segment |
| **New category** (e.g., "outcome-based project coordination") | Define the problem and solution from scratch | Requires market education; expensive | Only when existing categories genuinely don't describe your value |

## Positioning Statement

Combine all 5 components into a single positioning statement:

**Template:**
> For [best-fit customer] who [specific situation from Component 4], [product name] is the [market category from Component 5] that [primary value theme from Component 3]. Unlike [primary alternative from Component 1], we [unique attribute from Component 2].

**Your positioning statement:**
> For [___________] who [___________], [product name] is the [___________] that [___________]. Unlike [___________], we [___________].

**Example:**
> For ops managers at 10–100 person SaaS companies who spend more than 5 hours per week manually reconciling vendor invoices, AcmePay is the accounts payable automation tool that reduces month-end close from 5 days to same-day. Unlike QuickBooks' manual import workflow, we connect directly to your vendor portals and auto-reconcile in real time.
