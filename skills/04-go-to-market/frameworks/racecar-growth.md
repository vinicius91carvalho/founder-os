# Racecar Growth Framework

> A mental model for designing a sustainable growth system — distinguishing between one-time launch tactics, repeatable growth engines, and amplification mechanisms. Based on Lenny Rachitsky's framework, adapted from Reforge curriculum.

---

## Framework Overview

**The core insight:** Most startup growth advice conflates three fundamentally different things:
1. **Kickstarts** — tactics that generate initial momentum (one-time, non-repeatable)
2. **Growth engines** — repeatable, compounding acquisition mechanisms
3. **Turbo boosts** — periodic amplification of existing engines

Mixing these up causes two common failure modes:
- Founders run kickstarts and call them growth engines ("our Product Hunt launch proves our growth strategy works")
- Founders skip kickstarts and try to build an engine immediately ("we're not doing press, we're focused on building the content engine")

The framework also includes the **chassis** (retention and onboarding) — the part of the car that determines whether new customers stay, which determines whether growth compounds.

## Component 1: Kickstarts

**What they are:** One-time tactics that generate a burst of attention, signups, or customers. They provide the initial momentum needed to start an engine — but they cannot sustain growth on their own.

**Characteristics of kickstarts:**
- One-time or low-repeatability
- High peak, then fade
- Cannot be the primary long-term growth strategy
- Often generate PR, social proof, and customer data that feeds engine building

**The 8 Most Effective Kickstarts:**

### K1: Product Hunt Launch

**What it is:** A coordinated launch on Product Hunt that generates a spike of attention from tech-adjacent audiences.
**Typical outcome:** 200–2,000 signups on launch day; 10–100 paying customers in launch week
**Longtail value:** SEO backlinks from PH profile, press mentions, "As Seen on Product Hunt" badge
**Who it works for:** B2B SaaS, developer tools, productivity apps
**Use it when:** Product is working but you need initial signal and social proof

### K2: Hacker News "Show HN"

**What it is:** A submission to Hacker News under the "Show HN" category — showing the community what you've built.
**Typical outcome:** 50–500 signups on the day of submission; high-quality technical feedback
**Longtail value:** SEO backlinks, developer community credibility
**Who it works for:** Developer tools, infrastructure products, technical B2B
**Use it when:** You want technical user acquisition and feedback from sophisticated users

### K3: Press Coverage (Earned Media)

**What it is:** A news story, profile piece, or product review in a publication your target customers read.
**Typical outcome:** Variable — TechCrunch can drive 5,000+ signups; industry trade press may drive 200–500 highly targeted signups
**Longtail value:** SEO authority from backlinks, reference in fundraising, customer credibility
**Who it works for:** Any product with a compelling founder story or novel category
**Use it when:** You have a story to tell — founder background, contrarian insight, or market timing narrative

### K4: Influencer Seeding

**What it is:** Getting your product into the hands of influencers (micro or macro) who have your customer as their audience.
**Typical outcome:** 100–5,000 signups per influencer post, depending on audience size and relevance
**Longtail value:** Social proof, ambassador relationship
**Who it works for:** Consumer products, B2B tools with community-oriented buyers
**Use it when:** You can identify 5–10 influencers whose audiences are your exact customer

### K5: Cold Outbound (Founder-Led)

**What it is:** The founder personally sends targeted cold emails or LinkedIn messages to 100–500 potential customers.
**Typical outcome:** 2–10% meeting rate; 20–30% meeting-to-customer conversion; typically 5–30 customers from first outbound campaign
**Longtail value:** Deep customer insight from conversations, early enterprise relationships
**Who it works for:** B2B products with identifiable customer segment
**Use it when:** You need paying customers before any organic growth channel works

### K6: Community Post (Problem-Solving Content)

**What it is:** A single, highly useful piece of content posted in a community where your customers congregate (Reddit, Slack, LinkedIn, specific forums).
**Typical outcome:** 50–500 signups from a well-performing post; 1–20 paying customers
**Longtail value:** Community reputation, potential for recurring engagement
**Who it works for:** Any product with an identifiable customer community
**Use it when:** You can write something genuinely useful about the problem your product solves

### K7: Partnership Launch

**What it is:** Co-launching with a complementary product that shares your customer but doesn't compete. They promote to their list; you promote to yours.
**Typical outcome:** 50–500 signups from partner's list
**Longtail value:** Ongoing co-marketing relationship, integration, shared customer intelligence
**Who it works for:** B2B products with natural integration partners
**Use it when:** You have at least 500+ subscribers of your own to make the exchange fair

### K8: Conference / Event Tabling

**What it is:** Physical presence at an industry conference where your customers attend.
**Typical outcome:** 20–200 qualified conversations; 5–30 customers
**Longtail value:** Network relationships, customer face-time that deepens relationships
**Who it works for:** B2B products where face-to-face trust matters
**Use it when:** Your customers attend predictable events and your product demos well in person

## Component 2: Growth Engines

**What they are:** Repeatable, scalable mechanisms that generate new customers as a predictable output of a process. Unlike kickstarts, engines compound — they get more efficient over time as they scale.

**Characteristics of growth engines:**
- Repeatable (can be executed systematically)
- Measurable with consistent inputs and outputs
- Scalable (more input → proportional more output)
- Often self-reinforcing (each customer can feed the next)

**The three growth engines:**

### Engine 1: Paid Acquisition Engine

**Mechanism:** Spend money → acquire customers → measure CAC and LTV → if LTV > CAC, spend more

**How it works:**
1. Identify the highest-converting paid channel (Google Ads, LinkedIn Ads, Facebook/Instagram, YouTube)
2. Build and test ad creative until you find a winner (≥ 3% CTR, ≥ 5% landing page conversion)
3. Measure blended CAC and payback period
4. If CAC payback < 12 months: scale spend. If > 24 months: don't scale yet

**Best for:** Products with high-intent search demand (people actively searching for a solution), high LTV ($500+ ACV), and measurable conversion funnel

**When NOT to use:** If CAC > LTV/3, a paid engine will destroy capital. Do not run paid ads before you have proven conversion data.

**Key metric:** Blended CAC → compare to LTV. Rule: CAC payback ≤ 18 months before scaling.

**Example companies:** Many SaaS businesses with established search demand (Shopify, HubSpot, Zoom early)

---

### Engine 2: Virality Engine

**Mechanism:** Each new customer generates some fraction of a new customer through their usage

**Two types of virality:**

*Product virality (K-factor > 0):*
- Each user, by using the product naturally, exposes it to potential new users
- Examples: Calendly (sharing a booking link), Loom (sharing a video), Figma (sharing a design)
- K-factor: the average number of new users each existing user brings in
- K > 1: exponential growth (rare)
- K 0.3–0.9: meaningful virality that reduces CAC significantly
- K < 0.1: negligible

*Referral virality (incentivized):*
- Users are given explicit incentive to refer others (cash, credit, free months)
- Examples: Dropbox (extra storage for referrals), Airbnb ($25 travel credit), Uber (free rides)
- Requires a product that genuinely delivers enough value that users are willing to stake their social reputation

**How to build:**

1. **Identify the natural sharing moment** in your product: When do users already feel like sharing? (Calendly: sending a meeting link. Notion: sharing a doc. Loom: sharing a video)
2. **Make sharing frictionless:** One click. Beautiful sharing preview. Value communicated in the shared artifact itself
3. **Track K-factor:** Every cohort of N customers should generate K×N new users through sharing

**Best for:** Collaboration tools, communication tools, tools that create shareable artifacts

**When NOT to use:** Products that are inherently private (personal finance, HR tools, sensitive workflows) have natural virality ceilings

---

### Engine 3: Content / SEO Engine

**Mechanism:** Create content → rank for search queries your customers use → attract organic traffic → convert to customers

**How it works:**
1. Identify the keywords your customers search when experiencing the problem your product solves (not when searching for your product — when having the problem)
2. Create the best content on the internet for those queries
3. Build backlinks to increase domain authority
4. Convert organic traffic to signups via in-content CTAs and gated resources

**Compounding effect:** Unlike paid, content compounds. An article written in Month 3 still drives traffic in Year 3. This is the engine's primary advantage.

**Best for:** Products in categories with high search demand, B2B products where buyers research before buying, any product where customers actively search for solutions

**Timeline:** Slow to start (4–6 months before significant traffic). Compounding after 12–18 months.

**Key metric:** Organic traffic MoM growth, keyword ranking improvements, organic-sourced customer conversion rate

**Example companies:** HubSpot (content marketing category), Ahrefs (SEO tool content), Zapier (integration guides for every tool combination), Notion (template library + use case guides)

## Component 3: Turbo Boosts

**What they are:** Periodic, repeatable amplification mechanisms that temporarily increase the output of an existing engine. They don't replace engines — they temporarily multiply them.

**Characteristics:**
- Higher impact than daily engine operations
- Not sustainable as a daily activity
- Most effective when the underlying engine is healthy

**Common turbo boosts:**

| Turbo Boost | Engine it amplifies | Frequency | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Product announcement / new feature launch | All engines | Every 4–8 weeks | Notion's AI feature launch drove massive press + viral sharing |
| End-of-year promotion / seasonal pricing | Paid acquisition | 2–4× per year | 20% discount in November/December for annual plans |
| Large content piece / research report | SEO engine | Monthly | "State of [Industry]" report that earns 50+ backlinks |
| Category-creating PR moment | All engines | Once or twice per year | Being quoted in a major publication as a category leader |
| Live event / webinar | Content + referral | Monthly to quarterly | Live Q&A with 200+ target customers |
| Strategic partnership announcement | All engines | 1–3× per year | Integration launch with a major platform |

## Component 4: The Chassis (Retention and Onboarding)

**Why the chassis matters:** A leaky bucket wastes every engine and kickstart. If you acquire 100 customers per month but churn 90 per month, you'll never reach scale — regardless of how good your engine is.

**The chassis = the retention and activation infrastructure that keeps customers long enough to get value.**

### Key chassis metrics:

| Metric | Measures | Target (B2B SaaS) |
|---|---|---|
| Time to First Value (TTFV) | How long until a new user first experiences the core value | < 10 minutes for PLG; < 1 week for high-touch |
| Day 1 Activation | % of signups who complete the core activation action on Day 1 | > 40% |
| Day 7 Retention | % of signups who return on Day 7 | > 25% |
| Month 1 → Month 2 Retention | % of Month 1 customers who pay in Month 2 | > 75% |
| Monthly Churn | % of customers who cancel per month | < 3% for B2B SaaS |

### Chassis red flags:

- Day 1 activation < 20%: Onboarding is too complex or time-to-value is too long
- Day 7 retention < 15%: Core value isn't being delivered in first week
- Month 1 → Month 2 retention < 60%: Product isn't delivering enough ongoing value to justify renewal

## How to Use the Framework

### Step 1: Choose your kickstarts (first 30 days)

Select 2–3 kickstarts that fit your product and target customer. Execute them with full focus.

**Your kickstarts:**
1. [Kickstart 1]: [When and how]
2. [Kickstart 2]: [When and how]
3. [Kickstart 3]: [When and how]

### Step 2: Identify your primary engine (days 30–90)

Based on kickstart data, identify which engine fits your product:
- If kickstart data shows high search intent and high-quality organic traffic → Content/SEO engine
- If product naturally spreads through use (users invite others or share outputs) → Virality engine
- If paid ads show CAC < LTV/3 with conversion data → Paid engine

**Your primary engine:** [Paid / Virality / Content]
**Evidence for this choice:** [What from kickstarts supports this choice?]
**60-day KPIs to validate engine is working:**
- Input KPI: [What you'll do — e.g., "publish 8 articles per month"]
- Output KPI: [What you expect — e.g., "2,000 organic visitors per month by Day 60"]
- Conversion KPI: [Customers from engine — e.g., "10 organic-sourced customers per month"]

### Step 3: Fix the chassis before scaling

**Before investing in scaling the engine, verify the chassis is working:**
- [ ] TTFV is < 10 minutes (or < your target)
- [ ] Day 7 retention is > 25%
- [ ] Month 1 → Month 2 retention is > 70%

If chassis metrics are below target, fix retention before scaling acquisition. Scaling a leaky bucket accelerates cash burn without building a durable business.

### Step 4: Add turbo boosts at scale

**Once the engine is generating > 20 customers per month consistently:**
- Identify 2 turbo boosts that amplify your specific engine
- Schedule them quarterly
- Measure the lift each turbo boost creates above the engine's baseline

## Framework Application: B2B SaaS Example

**Product:** Invoice automation tool for ops teams
**Stage:** 40 paying customers, $25K MRR

| Component | Selection | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Kickstarts used | Product Hunt, LinkedIn founder outbound, 3 community posts | B2B SaaS with LinkedIn-active buyers; community (r/operations) has high target density |
| Primary engine | SEO/Content engine | High search intent ("invoice reconciliation automation", "AP automation for startups" — 15K+ monthly combined); content is differentiated by founder domain expertise |
| Secondary engine | Virality (referral) | Customers frequently recommend to peers in same role — adding structured referral incentive |
| Turbo boosts | Quarterly "AP Automation for Growing Teams" webinar; annual "State of Finance Ops" report | Webinar generates 50–100 MQL per event; report earns backlinks and press |
| Chassis priority | Day 1 activation is 22% (below target) — fixing onboarding to surface first successful reconciliation within 10 minutes | Without fixing TTFV, content traffic doesn't convert; paid would waste budget |
